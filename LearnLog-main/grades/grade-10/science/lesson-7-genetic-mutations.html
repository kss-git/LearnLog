Introduction

A genetic mutation is a permanent change in the DNA sequence. Mutations are the ultimate source of all genetic variation and evolution, but they can also cause disease. Not all mutations are bad - some are beneficial, many are neutral, and some are harmful. Understanding mutations helps us comprehend both human diversity and genetic diseases.

What Causes Mutations?

Mutations can occur naturally or be induced by environmental factors:

Spontaneous Mutations

Occur naturally during DNA replication

  • DNA copying errors
  • Random chemical changes
  • Occur in ~1 in 10 million base pairs

Induced Mutations

Caused by external factors (mutagens)

  • UV radiation from sunlight
  • X-rays and gamma rays
  • Chemicals (tobacco smoke, benzene)
  • Viruses

Real-World Connection: This is why dermatologists recommend sunscreen! UV radiation can cause mutations in skin cells, potentially leading to skin cancer.

Types of Mutations

Point Mutations (Single Base Changes)

A change in just one DNA base pair can have varying effects:

1. Substitution (One base replaces another)

Original DNA: ...ATG-CAC-GTA...
Mutated DNA: ...ATG-TAC-GTA...

Types of Substitution Effects:

  • Silent Mutation: No change in protein (due to genetic code redundancy)
  • Missense Mutation: Different amino acid in protein
  • Nonsense Mutation: Creates a stop signal, protein is cut short

2. Insertion (Extra base added)

Original DNA: ...ATG-CAC-GTA...
Mutated DNA: ...ATG-CTAC-GTA...

Causes a frameshift - changes all amino acids downstream!

3. Deletion (Base removed)

Original DNA: ...ATG-CAC-GTA...
Mutated DNA: ...ATG-CAC-GTA...

Also causes a frameshift - usually very harmful!

Chromosomal Mutations (Large-Scale Changes)

These mutations affect larger segments of chromosomes or entire chromosomes:

Down Syndrome Example

Down syndrome is caused by having an extra copy of chromosome 21 (trisomy 21). Instead of the normal two copies, individuals have three copies. This chromosomal mutation affects physical development and cognitive ability. It occurs in about 1 in 700 births.

Mutations and Disease: Real Examples

Sickle Cell Anemia

A single point mutation changes one amino acid in hemoglobin:

  • Mutation: GAG → GTG (glutamic acid → valine)
  • Effect: Red blood cells become crescent-shaped
  • Symptoms: Pain, fatigue, organ damage
  • Interesting fact: Carriers (one mutated gene) have resistance to malaria!

Cystic Fibrosis

Deletion of three DNA bases causes this disease:

  • Mutation: Deletion removes one amino acid from CFTR protein
  • Effect: Thick mucus builds up in lungs and digestive system
  • Prevalence: Most common in people of European descent

Cancer and Mutations

Cancer occurs when mutations accumulate in genes that control cell division:

  • Tumor suppressor genes: Normally stop uncontrolled growth (e.g., p53)
  • Oncogenes: Promote cell division; mutations can make them overactive
  • Multiple hits: Usually requires several mutations over time

BRCA1 and BRCA2 Genes

Mutations in these tumor suppressor genes dramatically increase breast and ovarian cancer risk. Genetic testing can identify carriers, allowing for preventive monitoring or treatment. Actress Angelina Jolie famously underwent preventive surgery after testing positive for a BRCA1 mutation.

Beneficial and Neutral Mutations

Not all mutations are harmful! In fact, they're essential for evolution.

Beneficial Mutations:

  • Lactose Tolerance: A mutation allows some adults to digest milk - advantageous in dairy-farming cultures
  • HIV Resistance: Some people have a mutation (CCR5-Δ32) that makes them resistant to HIV infection
  • High-Altitude Adaptation: Tibetan populations have mutations helping them thrive at high elevations
  • Low LDL Cholesterol: Some mutations naturally lower "bad" cholesterol, reducing heart disease risk

Neutral Mutations:

Most mutations have no effect on survival or reproduction:

  • Occur in non-coding DNA (doesn't make proteins)
  • Silent mutations (don't change the amino acid)
  • Create genetic diversity used in DNA fingerprinting and ancestry tests

Somatic vs. Germline Mutations

Somatic Mutations

Where: Body cells (skin, liver, lung, etc.)

Inherited? NO - only affect individual

Example: Skin cancer from UV exposure

Germline Mutations

Where: Egg or sperm cells

Inherited? YES - passed to offspring

Example: Inherited BRCA mutations

Detecting and Testing for Mutations

Modern medicine can identify many genetic mutations:

Genetic Testing Methods:

  • DNA Sequencing: Reads entire genes to find mutations
  • PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction): Amplifies specific DNA regions for analysis
  • Karyotyping: Examines chromosomes under microscope to detect large-scale changes
  • Prenatal Testing: Amniocentesis or chorionic villus sampling can detect mutations before birth

Ethical Considerations

Genetic testing raises important questions:

  • Should you be tested for diseases with no cure?
  • Who has access to your genetic information?
  • Could genetic information lead to discrimination?
  • What do you do with information about disease risk?

In many countries, laws like GINA (Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act) protect against genetic discrimination in employment and insurance.

Practice Questions

  1. Explain the difference between a point mutation and a chromosomal mutation. Which is likely to have a larger effect?
  2. Why are frameshift mutations (insertions/deletions) usually more harmful than substitution mutations?
  3. How can the same mutation (sickle cell) be both harmful and beneficial depending on circumstances?
  4. If a mutation occurs in your skin cell from sun exposure, will your children inherit it? Explain.
  5. Research: What is the "Philadelphia chromosome" and what disease is it associated with?
  6. Design a public health campaign explaining why UV protection is important at the molecular level.

Key Takeaways

  • Mutations are permanent changes in DNA sequence caused by errors or environmental factors
  • Point mutations change single bases; chromosomal mutations affect larger DNA segments
  • Mutations can be beneficial, neutral, or harmful depending on their effects
  • Germline mutations are inherited; somatic mutations are not
  • Many genetic diseases result from specific mutations that can now be detected through genetic testing
  • Mutations are the source of genetic variation and drive evolution
  • Understanding mutations has important medical and ethical implications
