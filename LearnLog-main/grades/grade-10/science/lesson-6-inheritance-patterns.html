Lesson 6: Inheritance Patterns

How Traits Pass from Parents to Offspring

Home Grade 10 Science

Introduction

Have you ever wondered why you might have your mother's eyes or your father's height? Inheritance patterns explain how traits are passed from parents to children through genes. Understanding these patterns helps us predict traits in offspring and explains why family members share similarities.

Gregor Mendel: The Father of Genetics

In the 1860s, an Austrian monk named Gregor Mendel studied pea plants and discovered the fundamental laws of inheritance. His work with thousands of pea plants revealed predictable patterns in how traits are inherited.

Mendel's Key Discoveries:

  • Traits are controlled by "factors" (now called genes)
  • Organisms inherit two copies of each gene (one from each parent)
  • Some versions of genes are dominant, others are recessive
  • Genes are passed to offspring independently

Dominant and Recessive Alleles

Genes come in different versions called alleles. Some alleles are dominant (represented by capital letters) and mask the effects of recessive alleles (lowercase letters).

Dominant Allele

Only ONE copy needed to show the trait

Example: Brown eyes (B)

Recessive Allele

TWO copies needed to show the trait

Example: Blue eyes (b)

Genotype vs. Phenotype:

  • Genotype: The actual genes you have (BB, Bb, or bb)
  • Phenotype: The trait you display (brown or blue eyes)

Family Example: If both parents have brown eyes but carry a recessive blue eye allele (Bb), there's a 25% chance their child could have blue eyes (bb)!

Punnett Squares: Predicting Inheritance

A Punnett square is a diagram that helps us predict the probability of offspring inheriting specific traits. Let's see how it works!

Example: Eye Color Inheritance

Both parents are heterozygous for brown eyes (Bb). What are the chances their child will have blue eyes?

Results:

  • 25% BB - Homozygous dominant (brown eyes)
  • 50% Bb - Heterozygous (brown eyes)
  • 25% bb - Homozygous recessive (blue eyes)

Phenotype Ratio: 75% brown eyes, 25% blue eyes (3:1 ratio)

Common Inherited Traits in Humans

Many human traits follow simple dominant-recessive patterns:

Dominant Trait Recessive Trait
Brown eyes Blue or green eyes
Dark hair Blonde or red hair
Curly hair Straight hair
Widow's peak Straight hairline
Free earlobes Attached earlobes
Dimples No dimples

Note: Many traits are actually controlled by multiple genes (polygenic) and are more complex than simple dominant-recessive patterns suggest.

Genetic Diseases and Inheritance

Some genetic diseases follow predictable inheritance patterns. Understanding these patterns is crucial for genetic counseling and medical care.

Recessive Genetic Diseases

Both parents must be carriers (heterozygous) or affected for a child to inherit the disease:

  • Cystic Fibrosis: Affects mucus production in lungs and digestive system
  • Sickle Cell Anemia: Causes red blood cells to become crescent-shaped
  • Tay-Sachs Disease: Progressive neurological disorder

Carrier Parents Example

If both parents are carriers of cystic fibrosis (Cc):

  • 25% chance child is unaffected (CC)
  • 50% chance child is a carrier (Cc)
  • 25% chance child has cystic fibrosis (cc)

Dominant Genetic Diseases

Only one copy of the mutated gene is needed to cause the disease:

  • Huntington's Disease: Progressive brain disorder appearing in adulthood
  • Achondroplasia: Most common form of dwarfism

Important: Genetic counseling helps families understand their risk of passing on genetic diseases and make informed decisions about family planning.

Beyond Simple Inheritance

Not all traits follow simple dominant-recessive patterns:

Incomplete Dominance

Heterozygotes show a blend of both traits

Example: Red flower × White flower = Pink flower

Codominance

Both alleles are fully expressed

Example: AB blood type shows both A and B proteins

Polygenic Traits

Multiple genes control one trait

Example: Skin color, height, intelligence

Practice Problems

  1. Create a Punnett square for a cross between a homozygous dominant tall plant (TT) and a homozygous recessive short plant (tt). What percentage of offspring will be tall?
  2. If a man with dimples (Dd) marries a woman without dimples (dd), what are the chances their child will have dimples?
  3. Both parents are carriers for sickle cell anemia (Ss). What is the probability their child will have the disease?
  4. Explain why two brown-eyed parents can have a blue-eyed child, but two blue-eyed parents cannot have a brown-eyed child.
  5. Research your own family: Identify three traits you share with your parents. Are they likely dominant or recessive?

Key Takeaways

  • Traits are inherited through genes that come in different versions (alleles)
  • Dominant alleles mask recessive alleles in heterozygotes
  • Punnett squares help predict the probability of offspring traits
  • Genetic diseases can follow dominant or recessive inheritance patterns
  • Many human traits are more complex than simple Mendelian inheritance
  • Understanding inheritance helps us predict disease risk and plan for family health
