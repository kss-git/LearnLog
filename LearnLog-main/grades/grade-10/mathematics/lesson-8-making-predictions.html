1. Expected Value

What is Expected Value?

Expected value (EV) is the average outcome you would expect if you repeated an event many times. It helps us make decisions when outcomes are uncertain.

Expected Value = Σ (Probability of outcome × Value of outcome)
EV = P₁ × V₁ + P₂ × V₂ + P₃ × V₃ + ...

Key insight: Expected value tells us the long-term average, not what will happen in a single trial.

Example 1: Gaming Tournament Prize Money

You're entering an esports tournament with the following prize structure:

Placement Prize Your Probability
1st Place$1,00010% (0.10)
2nd Place$50015% (0.15)
3rd Place$25025% (0.25)
No Prize$050% (0.50)

Entry fee: $75

Calculating Expected Value:

EV = (0.10 × $1,000) + (0.15 × $500) + (0.25 × $250) + (0.50 × $0)
EV = $100 + $75 + $62.50 + $0
EV = $237.50

Expected profit = EV - Entry fee
Expected profit = $237.50 - $75 = $162.50

Decision: With an expected profit of $162.50, entering the tournament is statistically favorable. Over many tournaments with similar odds, you would average a profit of $162.50 per tournament.

2. Using Trends to Make Predictions

Trend Analysis

When data shows a clear pattern over time, we can extend that pattern to predict future values. This is called extrapolation.

  • Linear trend: Data increases/decreases at a constant rate
  • Exponential trend: Data increases/decreases at an accelerating rate
  • Seasonal trend: Data follows repeating patterns

Example 2: YouTube Channel Growth Prediction

A content creator's subscriber count over 6 months:

Month Subscribers Monthly Growth
January1,000-

March2,000+500
April2,500+500
May3,000+500

Making a Prediction:

The channel grows by approximately 500 subscribers per month (linear trend).

Prediction for July = June subscribers + Monthly growth
Prediction for July = 3,500 + 500 = 4,000 subscribers

Prediction for December (6 months later):
December = 3,500 + (6 × 500) = 6,500 subscribers

3. Probability in Decision Making

Example 3: Basketball Free Throw Strategy

Your team is down by 2 points with 10 seconds left. You're fouled and get 2 free throws. Historical data shows:

  • Player A makes 85% of free throws
  • Player B makes 70% of free throws

Scenario Analysis:

If Player A shoots both:

P(Make both) = 0.85 × 0.85 = 0.72 or 72%
P(Make exactly 1) = 2 × (0.85 × 0.15) = 0.26 or 26%
P(Miss both) = 0.15 × 0.15 = 0.02 or 2%

If Player B shoots both:

P(Make both) = 0.70 × 0.70 = 0.49 or 49%
P(Make exactly 1) = 2 × (0.70 × 0.30) = 0.42 or 42%
P(Miss both) = 0.30 × 0.30 = 0.09 or 9%

Decision: Player A should take the shots because there's a 72% chance of tying the game (making both) compared to only 49% for Player B.

4. Poll Predictions and Sampling

Making Predictions from Samples

We often can't survey everyone in a population, so we use a representative sample to make predictions about the whole group.

Predicted proportion = (Sample proportion) × (Total population)

Important: Larger samples generally give more accurate predictions.

Example 4: School Election Poll

There are 1,200 students in your school. A poll of 200 randomly selected students shows:

Candidate Votes in Sample Sample Percentage
Candidate A9547.5%
Candidate B7537.5%
Candidate C3015%

Predicting Total Votes:

Candidate A: 0.475 × 1,200 = 570 votes
Candidate B: 0.375 × 1,200 = 450 votes
Candidate C: 0.15 × 1,200 = 180 votes

Prediction: Based on the poll, Candidate A is likely to win with approximately 570 votes.

5. Limitations and Uncertainty

When Predictions Can Fail

  • Biased samples: Your sample doesn't represent the population
  • External changes: Conditions change after you collect data
  • Small sample size: Not enough data to be confident
  • Outliers: Unusual events skew predictions
  • Wrong model: Assuming linear growth when it's actually exponential

Golden Rule: Always state the assumptions behind your predictions and acknowledge uncertainty!

Example 5: App Download Prediction Gone Wrong

Scenario: An app had these daily downloads:

Day 1: 100, Day 2: 150, Day 3: 200, Day 4: 250

Linear prediction for Day 30:

Growth rate = 50 downloads/day
Prediction: 100 + (29 × 50) = 1,550 downloads

What actually happened: 380 downloads on Day 30

Why the prediction failed:

  • Initial growth was from friends/family (not sustainable)
  • No ongoing marketing after launch week
  • Assumed constant growth rate (unrealistic for most apps)

Lesson: Consider the context, not just the numbers. Early trends don't always continue!

6. Real-World Decision Making

Practice Problems

1. Loot Box Analysis:

A game offers a loot box for $5 with these possible rewards:

  • Common skin (worth $3): 60% chance
  • Rare skin (worth $10): 30% chance
  • Epic skin (worth $25): 8% chance
  • Legendary skin (worth $100): 2% chance

Calculate the expected value. Should you buy the box?

2. Weather Prediction:

Over the past 50 days, it rained on 15 days. Based on this data:

  • What's the probability it will rain tomorrow?
  • Out of the next 20 days, how many rainy days would you predict?

3. Streaming Service Growth:

A creator's average viewers per stream:

Week 1: 50, Week 2: 65, Week 3: 80, Week 4: 95, Week 5: 110

  • What's the weekly growth rate?
  • Predict viewers for Week 8
  • What assumptions are you making?

4. Survey Prediction:

Your school has 800 students. You survey 100 students about lunch preferences:

  • Pizza: 45 students
  • Burgers: 30 students
  • Salad: 15 students
  • Other: 10 students

How many pizzas should the cafeteria prepare daily? Explain your reasoning.

5. Investment Decision:

You have $100 to invest. Option A has a 70% chance of returning $130 and 30% chance of losing everything. Option B guarantees $105. Which is better based on expected value? What other factors might influence your decision?

Key Takeaways

  • Expected value helps make decisions when outcomes are uncertain
  • Trends can be used to predict future values, but context matters
  • Larger, representative samples give better predictions
  • Always consider limitations: bias, sample size, changing conditions
  • Mathematical predictions should be combined with common sense and domain knowledge
  • State your assumptions and acknowledge uncertainty in predictions
