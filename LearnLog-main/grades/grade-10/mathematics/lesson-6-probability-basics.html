1. Introduction to Probability

What is Probability?

Probability is a number between 0 and 1 that represents how likely an event is to occur.

  • P = 0: Impossible event (will never happen)
  • P = 0.5: Equally likely to happen or not happen
  • P = 1: Certain event (will definitely happen)
Probability = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of possible outcomes

Example 1: Gaming Loot Box

A video game loot box contains:

  • 50 common items (gray)
  • 30 uncommon items (green)
  • 15 rare items (blue)
  • 4 epic items (purple)
  • 1 legendary item (gold)

Total items = 100

Calculate probabilities:

P(Common) = 50/100 = 0.50 or 50%
P(Uncommon) = 30/100 = 0.30 or 30%
P(Rare) = 15/100 = 0.15 or 15%
P(Epic) = 4/100 = 0.04 or 4%
P(Legendary) = 1/100 = 0.01 or 1%

What's the probability of getting at least a rare item (rare, epic, or legendary)?

P(At least rare) = P(Rare) + P(Epic) + P(Legendary)
= 0.15 + 0.04 + 0.01 = 0.20 or 20%

2. Experimental vs. Theoretical Probability

Two Types of Probability

Theoretical Probability: What we expect to happen based on mathematical reasoning

Experimental Probability: What actually happens when we conduct experiments

Experimental Probability = Number of times event occurred / Total number of trials

As the number of trials increases, experimental probability approaches theoretical probability (Law of Large Numbers).

Example 2: Free Throw Success Rate

A basketball player practices free throws. Here's their performance:

Practice Session Shots Made Total Shots Success Rate
Session 1 (small sample) 7 10 70%
Session 2 16 20 80%
Session 3 38 50 76%
Session 4 (large sample) 152 200 76%
Overall 213 280 76.1%

Conclusion: Based on experimental data, the player has about a 76% probability of making a free throw. This is their experimental probability, which stabilized after many attempts.

3. Compound Events

Probability Rules for Multiple Events

AND Rule (Independent Events):

P(A AND B) = P(A) × P(B)

Use when both events must happen.

OR Rule (Mutually Exclusive Events):

P(A OR B) = P(A) + P(B)

Use when either event can happen, but not both.

Complement Rule:

P(NOT A) = 1 - P(A)

The probability that an event doesn't happen.

Example 3: Gaming Streak Probability

A gamer wins 60% of their matches. What's the probability they win the next 3 matches in a row?

P(Win match 1) = 0.60
P(Win match 2) = 0.60
P(Win match 3) = 0.60

P(Win all 3) = 0.60 × 0.60 × 0.60
P(Win all 3) = 0.216 or 21.6%

What's the probability they lose at least one match?

P(Lose at least one) = 1 - P(Win all 3)
= 1 - 0.216 = 0.784 or 78.4%

Example 4: Rolling Dice - OR Probability

You roll a standard six-sided die. What's the probability of rolling a 5 OR a 6?

P(Roll 5) = 1/6
P(Roll 6) = 1/6

P(5 OR 6) = P(5) + P(6)
= 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3 ≈ 33.3%

These are mutually exclusive events (you can't roll both a 5 and 6 on one die), so we add the probabilities.

Key Takeaways

  • Probability always falls between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain)
  • For independent events happening together, multiply probabilities (AND rule)
  • For mutually exclusive events, add probabilities (OR rule)
  • Experimental probability approaches theoretical probability with more trials
  • Tree diagrams help visualize all possible outcomes in compound events
  • The complement rule: P(not A) = 1 - P(A)
