Introduction to Advanced Derivatives

While basic derivatives measure instantaneous rates of change, advanced derivative techniques allow us to analyze complex relationships, model physical systems, and solve problems where variables are interconnected. This lesson explores implicit differentiation, related rates, and higher-order derivatives with real-world applications in physics and engineering.

1. Implicit Differentiation

When equations define relationships implicitly (y cannot be easily isolated), we differentiate both sides with respect to x and solve for dy/dx.

Example: Circle Equation

Find dy/dx for the circle x² + y² = 25

Solution:

Differentiate both sides: 2x + 2y(dy/dx) = 0

Solve for dy/dx: dy/dx = -x/y

At point (3, 4): dy/dx = -3/4

Application: Elliptical Orbit

For a planet in an elliptical orbit described by x²/a² + y²/b² = 1, the instantaneous velocity direction is perpendicular to the radius vector, found using implicit differentiation.

2. Related Rates

Related rates problems involve finding how fast one quantity changes by using information about how fast related quantities change.

Example: Expanding Balloon

A spherical balloon is being inflated at 100 cm³/s. How fast is the radius increasing when r = 5 cm?

Solution:

Volume of sphere: V = (4/3)πr³

Differentiate with respect to time: dV/dt = 4πr²(dr/dt)

Given: dV/dt = 100 cm³/s, r = 5 cm

100 = 4π(25)(dr/dt)

dr/dt = 100/(100π) = 1/π ≈ 0.318 cm/s

Application: Radar Tracking

A car travels east at 80 km/h. A police radar 20 km north of the road tracks the car. How fast is the distance between the car and radar changing when the car is 15 km past the radar's latitude?

Solution:

Let x = horizontal distance from radar's latitude, d = distance to car

By Pythagorean theorem: d² = x² + 20²

Differentiate: 2d(dd/dt) = 2x(dx/dt)

When x = 15: d = √(225 + 400) = 25 km

Given dx/dt = 80 km/h:

dd/dt = (15 × 80)/25 = 48 km/h

3. Higher-Order Derivatives

The second derivative (d²y/dx²) measures the rate of change of the rate of change, indicating concavity and acceleration.

Physical Interpretation

  • Position: s(t) - location of object
  • Velocity: v(t) = s'(t) - rate of change of position
  • Acceleration: a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t) - rate of change of velocity
  • Jerk: j(t) = a'(t) = s'''(t) - rate of change of acceleration

Example: Projectile Motion

A ball is thrown upward with position s(t) = -4.9t² + 20t + 2 meters (where t is in seconds).

Analysis:

Velocity: v(t) = s'(t) = -9.8t + 20 m/s

Acceleration: a(t) = s''(t) = -9.8 m/s² (gravity)

Maximum height at v(t) = 0: -9.8t + 20 = 0 → t ≈ 2.04 seconds

Maximum height: s(2.04) ≈ 22.4 meters

Concavity and Inflection Points

The second derivative determines the shape of a curve:

  • f''(x) > 0: Concave up (curve bends upward)
  • f''(x) < 0: Concave down (curve bends downward)
  • f''(x) = 0: Possible inflection point (concavity changes)

4. Applications in Economics

Marginal Analysis

In economics, derivatives represent marginal quantities:

  • Marginal Cost: C'(x) - cost of producing one more unit
  • Marginal Revenue: R'(x) - revenue from selling one more unit
  • Marginal Profit: P'(x) = R'(x) - C'(x)

Example: Production Optimization

Cost function: C(x) = 1000 + 50x + 0.1x² dollars (x units produced)

Revenue function: R(x) = 100x - 0.2x² dollars

Find optimal production level:

Profit: P(x) = R(x) - C(x) = -1000 + 50x - 0.3x²

Marginal profit: P'(x) = 50 - 0.6x

Maximum when P'(x) = 0: x ≈ 83 units

Verify with second derivative: P''(x) = -0.6 < 0 (confirms maximum)

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Implicit Differentiation

Find dy/dx for x³ + y³ = 6xy

Problem 2: Related Rates

A 5-meter ladder leans against a wall. The bottom slides away at 0.5 m/s. How fast is the top sliding down when the bottom is 3 meters from the wall?

Problem 3: Motion Analysis

An object's position is s(t) = t³ - 9t² + 24t meters. Find when the object is momentarily at rest and determine if it's speeding up or slowing down at t = 4 seconds.

Problem 4: Optimization

A farmer has 200 meters of fence to enclose a rectangular field along a river (one side needs no fence). What dimensions maximize the area?

Key Takeaways

  • Implicit differentiation handles equations where y cannot be easily isolated
  • Related rates connect the rates of change of multiple variables
  • Higher-order derivatives reveal acceleration, concavity, and optimization
  • Second derivative test: f''(x) > 0 indicates local minimum, f''(x) < 0 indicates local maximum
  • Applications span physics (motion), economics (marginal analysis), and engineering (optimization)
