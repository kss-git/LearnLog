Lesson 5: Sentence Structure

Building Clear and Effective Sentences

The Four Types of Sentence Structures

1. Simple Sentences

One independent clause with a subject and predicate.

Subject Verb
Text Message:

"Just arrived" → I just arrived.

Complete Simple Sentences:
  • The email arrived.
  • Sarah posted a new story.
  • My phone died during the meeting.

2. Compound Sentences

Two or more independent clauses joined by a conjunction or semicolon.

Independent Clause Conjunction Independent Clause
Social Media Fail:

"I sent the email I didn't hear back"

Fixed:

I sent the email, but I didn't hear back.

I sent the email; I didn't hear back.

Pro Tip: Use FANBOYS to remember coordinating conjunctions: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So

3. Complex Sentences

One independent clause + one or more dependent clauses.

Email Mistake:

"Because I was late the meeting started without me"

Professional Version:

Because I was late, the meeting started without me.

The meeting started without me because I was late.

Common Subordinating Conjunctions:

Time: when, while, before, after, until, since

Cause: because, since, as

Condition: if, unless, whether

Contrast: although, though, even though, whereas

4. Compound-Complex Sentences

Multiple independent clauses + at least one dependent clause.

Professional Email Example:

Although the server crashed, I recovered the data, and the presentation went smoothly.

Although the server crashed (dependent),

I recovered the data (independent),

and the presentation went smoothly (independent).

Common Sentence Structure Mistakes

Fragments (Incomplete Sentences)

Text Fail:

"Walking to the store."

"Because I said so."

"After the meeting ended."

Complete Sentences:

I am walking to the store.

You should do it because I said so.

We grabbed lunch after the meeting ended.

Run-on Sentences

Social Media Fail:

"I need to finish this report it's due tomorrow I haven't even started the research this is bad"

Fixed Options:

I need to finish this report. It's due tomorrow, and I haven't even started the research. This is bad.

I need to finish this report; it's due tomorrow. I haven't even started the research, which is bad.

Comma Splices

Email Mistake:

"The client called, they want a refund, I don't know what to do."

Professional Fix:

The client called. They want a refund, and I don't know what to do.

The client called; they want a refund. I don't know what to do.

Real-World Applications

Context Best Structure Example
Quick text message Simple "Running 10 minutes late."
Professional email Complex/Compound "Although I'm still waiting for approval, I've prepared the documents, and we can proceed once confirmed."
Social media post Simple/Compound "Just finished the project, and I'm exhausted but happy!"
Formal report Compound-Complex "While sales increased by 15%, costs rose simultaneously, so profit margins remained stable."

Practice Exercise

Fix these common texting/social media mistakes:

  1. "cant make it tonight something came up sorry"
  2. "The wifi is down I can't join the zoom call I'm trying to hotspot"
  3. "Because the file was too large."
  4. "Meeting at 3, bring your laptop, we'll work on the presentation"
Show Answers
  1. "I can't make it tonight. Something came up. Sorry!"
  2. "The wifi is down, so I can't join the Zoom call. I'm trying to hotspot."
  3. "I couldn't send it because the file was too large."
  4. "The meeting is at 3. Bring your laptop, and we'll work on the presentation."
Remember: Good sentence structure makes your writing clear and professional. Whether you're texting a friend or emailing your boss, complete sentences show you care about communication!
