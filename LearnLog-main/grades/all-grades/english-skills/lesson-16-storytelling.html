📚 What You'll Learn

Storytelling is the ancient art of taking readers on a journey. Whether you're writing a short fanfic, a game narrative, or a full novel, you need structure, characters, conflict, and resolution.

Think of story structure like a game level: tutorial (setup), challenges (rising action), boss fight (climax), victory screen (resolution)!

💡 Core Concept 1: The Three-Act Structure

The foundation of most stories. Like levels in a game, each act has a specific job.

Act 1: Setup (25% of story)

What happens: Introduce world, characters, and normal life

Key Elements:

  • Hook: Grab attention immediately
  • Normal World: Show what life is like before adventure
  • Inciting Incident: The event that changes everything
  • First Plot Point: Hero commits to the journey

Example (Star Wars): Luke's boring farm life → Leia's message → Obi-Wan's offer → Luke's home destroyed → He joins Obi-Wan

Act 2: Confrontation (50% of story)

What happens: Hero faces obstacles, learns, grows, struggles

Key Elements:

  • Rising Action: Challenges get progressively harder
  • Midpoint: Major revelation or setback (game-changer!)
  • Complications: Things go wrong, stakes increase
  • Crisis/Low Point: Everything seems lost (darkest moment)

Example (Star Wars): Training → Rescuing Leia → Escape attempts → Obi-Wan dies (low point)

Act 3: Resolution (25% of story)

What happens: Final confrontation and aftermath

Key Elements:

  • Climax: The big showdown/boss fight
  • Resolution: Wrap up loose ends
  • Denouement: New normal (how has the world changed?)

Example (Star Wars): Trench run → Death Star destroyed → Medal ceremony (new normal: Luke is a hero)

💡 Core Concept 2: Creating Compelling Characters

Characters drive stories. Readers care about PEOPLE, not just plot.

The Character Formula

Every great character needs:

1. WANT (External Goal): What they're trying to achieve

Example: Frodo wants to destroy the ring

2. NEED (Internal Goal): What they actually need to grow/heal

Example: Frodo needs to overcome fear and become brave

3. FLAW/WEAKNESS: What holds them back

Example: Frodo is small, inexperienced, tempted by power

4. STAKES: What happens if they fail

Example: Middle-earth falls to darkness

Character Arc (Growth Over Time)

Positive Arc: Character overcomes flaw and grows

Example: Zuko (Avatar) goes from villain to hero

Negative Arc: Character succumbs to flaw and falls

Example: Anakin becomes Darth Vader

Flat Arc: Character stays the same but changes the world around them

Example: Captain America maintains his values while others change

Making Characters Feel Real

  • Give them quirks: Unique habits, speech patterns, interests
  • Contradictions: A tough fighter who loves knitting, a genius who's socially awkward
  • Backstory: Past events that shaped who they are (but don't dump it all at once!)
  • Relationships: How they interact with others reveals character
  • Flaws: Perfect characters are BORING. Give them weaknesses!

💡 Core Concept 3: Conflict is Everything

No conflict = No story. Stories ARE about problems and how characters solve them.

Types of Conflict

1. Character vs. Character

Hero vs. Villain, rivals, opposing goals

Example: Harry vs. Voldemort, Batman vs. Joker

2. Character vs. Self

Internal struggle, doubt, temptation, identity crisis

Example: Frodo vs. Ring's corruption, Spider-Man's responsibility

3. Character vs. Society

Against social norms, government, system

Example: The Hunger Games, 1984, Avatar (blue people vs. humans)

4. Character vs. Nature

Survival, natural disasters, animals

Example: The Revenant, Minecraft survival mode, Hatchet

5. Character vs. Technology

AI, robots, machines taking over

Example: The Matrix, Portal, Terminator

6. Character vs. Supernatural

Magic, gods, fate, destiny

Example: Percy Jackson vs. gods, Supernatural vs. demons

Escalating Conflict (Make it Worse!)

Every time your hero solves one problem, create a BIGGER problem.

Example progression:

  1. Hero needs to find a key
  2. Key is guarded by a monster
  3. Defeating monster triggers an alarm
  4. Guards arrive, hero is trapped
  5. Hero escapes but friend is captured
  6. Now hero must rescue friend AND complete original mission

The rule: If things are going smoothly, you're doing it wrong!

💡 Core Concept 4: Planning vs. Discovery

Two approaches to writing - both work!

Plotters (Planners)

Method: Outline everything before writing

Pros:

  • Know where you're going
  • Less likely to get stuck
  • Easier to plant foreshadowing
  • Better pacing control

Tools: Beat sheets, chapter outlines, character profiles

Pantsers (Discovery Writers)

Method: Start writing and discover the story as you go

Pros:

  • More spontaneous and creative
  • Characters feel more alive
  • Can surprise yourself
  • Less pressure upfront

Cons: Might write yourself into corners, need more revision

Plantser (Hybrid - Best of Both!)

Method: Plan the major beats, discover the details

Example plan:

  • Know your beginning, middle climax, and ending
  • Know your main character's arc
  • Wing the specifics of HOW you get there

Why it works: Structure prevents getting lost, flexibility keeps it fun!

✏️ Example: Building a Short Story

Let's plan a 2000-word fanfic using the three-act structure:

Premise: A Minecraft player discovers a mysterious portal that wasn't there before.

ACT 1 (500 words - 25%):

  • Opening: Alex is mining for diamonds in their usual spot
  • Normal world: Routine mining, almost have enough for armor
  • Inciting incident: Wall breaks, reveals glowing purple portal (NOT nether portal)
  • First plot point: Alex hears a voice calling for help from the portal, decides to enter

ACT 2 (1000 words - 50%):

  • New world: Strange dimension, blocks are different, mobs are corrupted
  • Meet ally: Steve, trapped here for years, explains the portal is collapsing
  • Rising action: They need three ancient crystals to stabilize it
  • Midpoint: Find first crystal, but activating it alerts a boss mob
  • Complications: Boss is too strong, they retreat
  • Low point: Steve reveals he tried this before, his team died. Maybe it's hopeless.
  • Decision: Alex refuses to give up, has an idea using redstone mechanics

ACT 3 (500 words - 25%):

  • Climax: Epic boss fight using redstone trap Alex built
  • Victory: Boss defeated, portal stabilizes
  • Resolution: Steve can go home, both players escape
  • New normal: Back in regular Minecraft, but Alex has new enchanted gear from that dimension
  • Final line: "Alex smiled and placed the crystal on their mantle. Some adventures are worth the risk."

Why this works: Clear structure, character arc (Alex learns bravery), escalating conflict, satisfying resolution!

🎯 Practice Exercises

Exercise 1: Story in Three Sentences

Summarize any story (movie, game, book) in exactly three sentences - one for each act:

  • Sentence 1: Setup (who, where, inciting incident)
  • Sentence 2: Confrontation (challenges, midpoint, crisis)
  • Sentence 3: Resolution (climax and outcome)

Example (Finding Nemo):

1) Overprotective clownfish Marlin's son Nemo is captured by divers.

2) Marlin journeys across the ocean facing sharks, jellyfish, and his own fears, while Nemo plots escape from a fish tank.

3) Father and son reunite and escape together, with Marlin learning to let Nemo be independent.

Exercise 2: Create a Character

Design a character with:

  • Name and basic description
  • External want (what they're trying to achieve)
  • Internal need (what they must learn/overcome)
  • Major flaw
  • One unique quirk or habit

Exercise 3: Escalate the Conflict

Starting scenario: "A student forgot their homework and the teacher is collecting it."

Make it worse 5 times:

  1. Student tries to fake it but...
  2. That makes things worse because...
  3. Now they also have to deal with...
  4. Just when they think it can't get worse...
  5. Finally, the ultimate complication...

Exercise 4: Plan Your Own Story

Outline a short story (1000-2000 words) using the three-act structure. Include:

  • One-sentence premise
  • Main character with want, need, and flaw
  • Key plot points for each act
  • Type(s) of conflict
  • How the character changes by the end

🚀 Pro Storytelling Tips

  • Start late, end early: Begin as close to the action as possible, end soon after climax
  • Kill your darlings: If a scene doesn't advance plot or character, cut it
  • Raise the stakes: Make failure have real consequences the reader cares about
  • Subvert expectations: Occasionally surprise readers, but don't be random
  • Plant and payoff: Setups in Act 1 pay off in Act 3 (Chekhov's gun)
  • Theme matters: What's your story REALLY about? (love, courage, sacrifice, etc.)
  • End with change: Characters or world should be different by the end
  • Read widely: Study stories in books, games, movies - see what works

⚠️ Common Storytelling Mistakes

1. Sagging Middle

Problem: Act 2 drags with no clear direction

Fix: Add a strong midpoint that changes everything, keep escalating conflict

2. Deus Ex Machina (Convenient Solutions)

Problem: Random power/character/event saves the day out of nowhere

Fix: Plant solutions earlier, make heroes solve problems with established skills

3. Flat Characters

Problem: Characters don't grow or change

Fix: Give them an arc - they should be different at the end

4. Predictable Plot

Problem: Readers see every twist coming

Fix: Subvert ONE major expectation, but keep structure familiar

5. Too Many Characters

Problem: Readers can't keep track of everyone

Fix: Focus on 1-3 main characters, give others clear distinctive traits

📋 Story Planning Worksheet

Use this checklist before you start writing:

Premise & Hook

  • ☐ Can I summarize my story in one sentence?
  • ☐ Is the hook interesting enough to grab readers immediately?

Character

  • ☐ What does my protagonist WANT (external goal)?
  • ☐ What do they NEED (internal growth)?
  • ☐ What is their major FLAW?
  • ☐ How will they CHANGE by the end?

Conflict

  • ☐ What type(s) of conflict am I using?
  • ☐ What are the stakes (what happens if hero fails)?
  • ☐ Does conflict escalate throughout?

Structure

  • ☐ Act 1: What's the inciting incident?
  • ☐ Act 2: What's the midpoint game-changer?
  • ☐ Act 2: What's the low point/crisis?
  • ☐ Act 3: What's the climax?
  • ☐ Act 3: How does it end (new normal)?

Polish

  • ☐ Is there a clear theme/message?
  • ☐ Have I planted setups that will pay off?
  • ☐ Does every scene advance plot or character?
