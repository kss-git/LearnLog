By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Balance dialogue with narration and action

📚 What You'll Learn

Dialogue is more than just characters talking - it's a tool to reveal personality, advance plot, create tension, and build relationships. Good dialogue sounds natural but is actually carefully crafted.

Think of dialogue like voice acting: every character needs their own voice, rhythm, and way of speaking!

🌟 Why This Matters

Dialogue skills are essential for:

  • Fanfiction: Keeping characters true to their canon voices
  • Game Writing: Creating memorable NPC conversations and quest dialogue
  • Screenwriting: Making scripts that actors want to perform
  • Comics/Manga: Fitting impactful dialogue into speech bubbles
  • Social Skills: Understanding conversation flow helps in real life too!

Bad dialogue pulls readers out of the story. Great dialogue makes characters unforgettable!

💡 Core Concept 1: Writing Natural Dialogue

The Golden Rules

1. People don't speak in perfect sentences

❌ "I am going to the store because we are out of milk."

✅ "Gonna grab milk. We're out."

2. Use contractions

❌ "I cannot believe you did not tell me!"

✅ "I can't believe you didn't tell me!"

Exception: Formal characters or emphasis ("I will NOT let you go!")

3. Add interruptions and trailing off

✅ "I was thinking we could—"

"No."

"But you didn't even—"

"I said no."

4. Show emotion through word choice

Angry: "Whatever. I don't care." (short, clipped)

Nervous: "I mean, like, maybe we could, um, try?" (fillers, hesitation)

Excited: "Omg omg omg did you see that?!" (fast, enthusiastic)

💡 Core Concept 2: Dialogue Tags & Action Beats

Dialogue Tags (Said, Asked, etc.)

What they are: Words that identify who's speaking

The "said" rule: Use "said" 90% of the time. It's invisible to readers!

✅ "I don't believe you," she said.

✅ "Are you sure?" he asked.

⚠️ "I hate this!" she exclaimed. (okay occasionally)

❌ "Never!" he retorted vehemently. (trying too hard)

Action Beats (Better Than Tags!)

What they are: Physical actions that replace dialogue tags

Benefits: Show emotion, add movement, feel more dynamic

Compare:

⚠️ "I'm fine," she said angrily.

✅ "I'm fine." She slammed the door.

⚠️ "Maybe we should go," he said nervously.

✅ "Maybe we should go." He glanced at the exit.

When to Use Nothing At All

In back-and-forth exchanges between two characters, you can drop tags entirely:

"Did you hear that?"

"Yeah. What was it?"

"I don't know, but I don't like it."

"Should we check it out?"

"Are you crazy?"

Tip: Every 3-4 lines, add a tag or action beat so readers don't lose track!

💡 Core Concept 3: Dialogue Formatting Rules

Basic Punctuation

Comma before closing quote (with tag):

✅ "I'm going home," she said.

❌ "I'm going home." she said.

Period inside closing quote (no tag):

✅ "I'm going home." She grabbed her bag.

Question marks and exclamation points stay:

✅ "Are you serious?" he asked.

✅ "Look out!" She shoved him aside.

Multi-Paragraph Dialogue

When one character speaks for multiple paragraphs, don't close quotes until they're done:

"Let me tell you what happened. It was crazy.

"First, the power went out. Then we heard this noise from upstairs.

"That's when I knew we had to get out of there."

Note: Opening quote each paragraph, closing quote only at the end.

Interrupted Dialogue

Em dash for interruption:

"I was just trying to—"

"I don't care what you were trying to do!"

Ellipsis for trailing off:

"Maybe if we just..."

"Just what?"

"I don't know. Never mind."

✏️ Example: Rewriting Dialogue

Scenario: Two friends discover a secret room in a video game.

❌ Bad Version (Stiff, Over-Tagged):

"I think I have found something interesting," Alex said excitedly.

"What is it?" Jordan inquired curiously.

"It appears to be a hidden door," Alex explained enthusiastically.

"We should open it," Jordan suggested eagerly.

Problems: Formal speech, adverb overload, repetitive tags

✅ Good Version (Natural, Dynamic):

"Yo, check this out!"

Jordan leaned closer to the screen. "Is that—?"

"A hidden door." Alex's fingers flew across the controller. "I'm opening it."

"Wait, shouldn't we—"

The door swung open.

"Never mind."

Improvements: Natural speech, action beats, interruptions, varied rhythm

🎯 Practice Exercises

Exercise 1: Give It Voice

Rewrite this bland dialogue with character personality:

"We should go to the party." "Okay, that sounds good." "What time?" "Seven o'clock."

Make one character excited and one reluctant.

Exercise 2: Tag or Beat?

Replace the dialogue tags with action beats:

"I can't do this anymore," she said sadly. "Please don't give up," he said hopefully.

Exercise 3: Fanfic Challenge

Pick two characters from your favorite show/game/book. Write a dialogue between them where they:

  • Discover something surprising
  • Disagree about what to do
  • Make a decision together

Challenge: Make their voices so accurate that another fan would recognize them!

🚀 Pro Tips from Game/Film Writers

  • Read it aloud: If it sounds weird when you say it, it'll sound weird when readers read it
  • Less is more: Cut 20% of your dialogue - you'll be surprised how much better it gets
  • Subtext is king: Best dialogue is about what's NOT being said
  • Avoid exposition dumps: Characters shouldn't explain things they both already know
  • Use silence: Sometimes a pause or a look says more than words
  • Conflict makes it interesting: Even friendly conversations should have slight tension
  • Watch with subtitles: Study how your favorite shows write dialogue

⚠️ Common Dialogue Mistakes

1. The "As You Know, Bob" Problem

❌ "As you know, Bob, we've been best friends since kindergarten and we both love skateboarding."

Why it's bad: Bob already knows this! It's forced exposition.

✅ Instead, show this through natural conversation and action.

2. Everyone Sounds the Same

Fix: Give each character unique speech patterns, vocabulary, and sentence structure.

3. Too Much Small Talk

❌ "Hi." "Hi." "How are you?" "Good, you?" "Good." "Nice weather." "Yeah."

Fix: Skip the boring parts. Start conversations mid-conflict or mid-action.

4. Adverb Abuse

❌ "I hate you!" she said angrily. (The dialogue already shows anger!)

✅ "I hate you!" She slammed her fist on the table.

📋 Quick Reference Checklist

Before you finish your dialogue scene, check:

  • ☐ Does each character sound different?
  • ☐ Could I remove any dialogue tags and still know who's speaking?
  • ☐ Am I using "said" most of the time?
  • ☐ Do I have action beats to show emotion?
  • ☐ Is the dialogue natural when read aloud?
  • ☐ Did I avoid exposition dumps?
  • ☐ Is there subtext (characters not saying everything)?
  • ☐ Do characters interrupt, trail off, or hesitate naturally?
  • ☐ Is my punctuation correct?
  • ☐ Does the dialogue advance plot or reveal character?
