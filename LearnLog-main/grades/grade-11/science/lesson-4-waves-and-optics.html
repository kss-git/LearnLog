What You'll Learn

Waves are disturbances that transfer energy through space without transferring matter. From ocean waves to sound waves to light waves, the same fundamental principles apply.

Optics is the study of light behavior - how it reflects, refracts, and interacts with materials. Understanding optics explains everything from eyeglasses to fiber optic internet.

Why This Matters

Waves and optics power modern life:

  • Telecommunications: Fiber optics transmit internet data as light pulses at 200,000 km/s
  • Audio Engineering: Concert halls designed using wave interference and resonance principles
  • Medical Imaging: Ultrasound uses sound waves to visualize internal organs
  • Wireless Tech: WiFi, Bluetooth, cell phones all use electromagnetic waves
  • Music Production: Understanding frequencies and harmonics creates better sound

Core Concept: Wave Properties

Wavelength (λ)

Distance between successive wave peaks. Measured in meters.

Frequency (f)

Number of complete waves passing a point per second. Measured in Hertz (Hz = cycles/second).

Amplitude (A)

Maximum displacement from equilibrium. Determines wave energy and intensity.

  • Sound: Larger amplitude = louder sound
  • Light: Larger amplitude = brighter light

Wave Speed (v)

How fast the wave disturbance propagates.

Universal Wave Equation: v = fλ

Speed = frequency × wavelength

Example: Sound at 440 Hz (musical note A) with wavelength 0.78 m travels at v = (440)(0.78) = 343 m/s

Visual: Wave Anatomy

Key insight: In a transverse wave, particle motion is perpendicular to wave direction (like water waves or light). In a longitudinal wave, particle motion is parallel to wave direction (like sound).

Wave Behaviors

1. Reflection

Wave bounces off a boundary. Angle of incidence = angle of reflection.

  • Sound: Echoes from walls
  • Light: Mirrors, shiny surfaces

2. Refraction

Wave bends when entering a different medium (changes speed).

  • Light: Pencil appears bent in water
  • Sound: Temperature layers bend sound waves

3. Interference

Two waves overlap and combine.

  • Constructive: Waves in phase add up (louder sound, brighter light)
  • Destructive: Waves out of phase cancel (quieter sound, dimmer light)
  • Application: Noise-canceling headphones use destructive interference

4. Diffraction

Waves bend around obstacles or through openings.

  • Why you can hear around corners but not see around them (sound wavelength >> light wavelength)

5. Doppler Effect

Observed frequency changes when source or observer moves.

  • Example: Ambulance siren sounds higher pitched approaching, lower pitched receding
  • Application: Police radar guns, astronomy (redshift/blueshift)

Visual: Total Internal Reflection in Fiber Optics

Key insight: When light hits the core-cladding boundary at an angle greater than the critical angle, 100% reflects back - no light escapes. This allows data to travel thousands of kilometers with minimal loss.

Optics: Refraction and Snell's Law

When light enters a different medium, it changes speed and bends.

Index of Refraction (n)

Measures how much a material slows down light.

Formula: n = c / v

Where c = speed of light in vacuum (3 × 10⁸ m/s), v = speed in material

Examples:

  • Air: n ≈ 1.00
  • Water: n = 1.33
  • Glass: n = 1.5
  • Diamond: n = 2.42

Snell's Law

Predicts the angle of refraction.

Formula: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂

Where n₁, n₂ are indices of refraction, θ₁, θ₂ are angles from the normal (perpendicular to surface)

Critical Angle and Total Internal Reflection

When light travels from higher n to lower n (e.g., glass to air), there's an angle beyond which all light reflects - none refracts out.

Critical angle formula: sin θ_c = n₂ / n₁

Example: Glass (n=1.5) to air (n=1): sin θ_c = 1/1.5 = 0.667 → θ_c = 42°

Any angle > 42° results in total internal reflection - this is how fiber optics work!

Example 1: Sound Wave Speed

Problem: A guitar string vibrates at 330 Hz, producing a sound wave with wavelength 1.04 m. What is the speed of sound?

Solution:

  1. Given:
    • f = 330 Hz
    • λ = 1.04 m
    • v = ?
  2. Apply wave equation:

    v = fλ

    v = (330)(1.04)

    v = 343 m/s

Answer: 343 m/s

(This is the speed of sound in air at 20°C)

Real-world insight: This is why you can tune instruments by frequency alone - for a given medium (air at room temperature), wavelength and frequency are locked together by v = fλ.

Example 2: Refraction in Water

Problem: Light traveling in air (n=1.00) hits water (n=1.33) at a 40° angle from the normal. What is the angle of refraction in the water?

Solution:

  1. Given:
    • n₁ = 1.00 (air)
    • θ₁ = 40°
    • n₂ = 1.33 (water)
    • θ₂ = ?
  2. Apply Snell's Law:

    n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂

    (1.00) sin 40° = (1.33) sin θ₂

    0.643 = 1.33 sin θ₂

    sin θ₂ = 0.643 / 1.33 = 0.483

    θ₂ = sin⁻¹(0.483) = 28.9°

Answer: 28.9°

Real-world insight: The light bends toward the normal when entering a denser medium (higher n). This is why pools look shallower than they are, and why spearfishing requires aiming below the apparent fish position.

Quick Check: Test Your Understanding

Question: An FM radio station broadcasts at 100 MHz (100 million Hz). If radio waves travel at the speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s), what is the wavelength?

Answer: 3 meters

Solution:

v = fλ → λ = v/f

λ = (3 × 10⁸) / (100 × 10⁶)

λ = 3 m

This is why FM radio antennas are a few meters long - they're designed to match the wavelength!

Practice Questions

Question 1 (Easy): A wave has frequency 50 Hz and wavelength 6 m. What is its speed?

Answer: 300 m/s

v = fλ = (50)(6) = 300 m/s

Question 2 (Medium): Sound travels at 343 m/s in air. What is the wavelength of a 686 Hz sound wave?

Answer: 0.5 m

v = fλ → λ = v/f = 343/686 = 0.5 m

Question 3 (Medium): What is the speed of light in glass with refractive index n = 1.5?

Answer: 2 × 10⁸ m/s

n = c/v → v = c/n = (3 × 10⁸)/1.5 = 2 × 10⁸ m/s

Question 4 (Hard): Light travels from water (n=1.33) to glass (n=1.5) at a 30° angle from normal. What is the refraction angle in glass?

Answer: 26.4°

Solution:

n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂

(1.33) sin 30° = (1.5) sin θ₂

0.665 = 1.5 sin θ₂

sin θ₂ = 0.443 → θ₂ = 26.4°

Question 5 (Hard): What is the critical angle for total internal reflection from glass (n=1.5) to air (n=1)?

Answer: 41.8°

Solution:

sin θ_c = n₂/n₁ = 1/1.5 = 0.667

θ_c = sin⁻¹(0.667) = 41.8°

Any angle > 41.8° results in total internal reflection.

Quick Summary

  • Wave equation: v = fλ (speed = frequency × wavelength)
  • Transverse waves: Perpendicular motion (light, water); Longitudinal: Parallel motion (sound)
  • Interference: Constructive (add) or destructive (cancel)
  • Doppler effect: Frequency shifts when source/observer moves
  • Snell's Law: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂ (predicts refraction)
  • Total internal reflection: Light traps in fiber optics when θ > θ_critical

Real-World Connection: Noise-Canceling Headphones

Active noise-canceling technology is destructive interference in action:

  • Microphones: Detect incoming ambient noise waves
  • Processing: Chip analyzes noise frequency and amplitude in real-time
  • Anti-Noise Generation: Creates sound waves exactly 180° out of phase (inverted)
  • Destructive Interference: Original noise + anti-noise = silence (waves cancel)

This works best for low-frequency, steady sounds (airplane engine hum, AC noise) because the processor can predict the wave pattern. Sudden, high-frequency sounds (clap, voice) are harder to cancel because they're unpredictable.

Premium headphones like Sony WH-1000XM series sample ambient noise 700+ times per second, adjusting anti-noise waves in milliseconds to maintain near-perfect cancellation.

Challenge Problem

The Fiber Optic Data Problem:

A fiber optic cable has a glass core (n = 1.48) surrounded by cladding (n = 1.45). The cable is 50 km long.

Part A: What is the critical angle for total internal reflection?

Part B: How long does it take a light pulse to travel the full 50 km cable?

Part C: How many reflections does light make if each bounce travels 2 mm along the cable before reflecting?

Hints:

Part A: Use sin θ_c = n_cladding / n_core

Part B: First find speed of light in glass core using n = c/v, then use distance/speed

Part C: Total distance / distance per reflection

Part A: Critical angle

sin θ_c = n₂/n₁ = 1.45/1.48 = 0.980

θ_c = sin⁻¹(0.980) = 78.6°

Answer: 78.6° from normal

Part B: Travel time

Speed in core: v = c/n = (3 × 10⁸)/1.48 = 2.03 × 10⁸ m/s

Distance: 50 km = 50,000 m

Time: t = d/v = 50,000 / (2.03 × 10⁸) = 2.46 × 10⁻⁴ s

Answer: 0.246 milliseconds (or 246 microseconds)

Part C: Number of reflections

Total distance: 50,000 m = 50,000,000 mm

Distance per reflection: 2 mm

Reflections: 50,000,000 / 2 = 25,000,000

Answer: 25 million reflections!

Real-world significance: Despite 25 million reflections, the signal arrives in under a millisecond with minimal loss because total internal reflection is 100% efficient (no light escapes). This is why your internet can travel across continents at near light-speed!
