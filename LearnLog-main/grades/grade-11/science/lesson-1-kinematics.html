Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Analyze motion using position-time and velocity-time graphs
  • Apply kinematic equations to solve real-world problems
  • Calculate displacement, velocity, and acceleration from motion data
  • Interpret the physical meaning of slopes and areas on motion graphs

What You'll Learn

Kinematics is the study of motion without considering the forces that cause it. Think of it as the language we use to describe how things move - whether it's a Formula 1 car accelerating on a straightaway, a basketball arcing through the air, or a phone dropping from your hand.

We'll focus on one-dimensional motion (movement along a straight line) and master the tools to predict exactly where an object will be at any moment.

Why This Matters

Kinematics is everywhere:

  • Crash Investigation: Police use kinematics to reconstruct accidents and determine fault
  • Self-Driving Cars: Tesla and Waymo calculate braking distances in milliseconds
  • Sports Analytics: Track sprint times, reaction speeds, and optimal acceleration
  • Space Exploration: NASA calculates rocket trajectories for Mars missions
  • Safety Engineering: Design airbag deployment timing and crumple zone effectiveness

Core Concept: The Big Three Variables

1. Position (x or d)

Position tells us where an object is relative to a reference point. Measured in meters (m).

Example: Your car is at the 150 m mark on the highway (with the on-ramp as the reference point).

2. Velocity (v)

Velocity is how fast position changes over time. It has both magnitude and direction. Measured in m/s.

Formula: v = Δx / Δt (change in position divided by change in time)

Example: A race car travels 200 m in 4 s → v = 200/4 = 50 m/s

3. Acceleration (a)

Acceleration is how fast velocity changes over time. Measured in m/s².

Formula: a = Δv / Δt (change in velocity divided by change in time)

Example: A car speeds up from 0 to 30 m/s in 6 s → a = 30/6 = 5 m/s²

Visual: Position-Time Graph

Key insight: The slope of a position-time graph gives you velocity. A steeper slope means faster motion. A curve means the object is accelerating.

Visual: Velocity-Time Graph

Key insight: The slope of a velocity-time graph gives you acceleration. The area under the curve gives you displacement.

The Kinematic Equations

When acceleration is constant, we can use these four powerful equations:

1. v = v₀ + at

Use when you know initial velocity, acceleration, and time.

2. x = x₀ + v₀t + ½at²

Use when you know initial position, velocity, acceleration, and time.

3. v² = v₀² + 2a(x - x₀)

Use when you don't know time (ideal for braking distance problems).

4. x = x₀ + ½(v₀ + v)t

Use when you know average velocity.

Variables:

  • x = final position, x₀ = initial position
  • v = final velocity, v₀ = initial velocity
  • a = acceleration
  • t = time

Example 1: Car Crash Analysis

Problem: A car is traveling at 30 m/s (about 108 km/h) when the driver sees an obstacle and hits the brakes. The car decelerates at -8 m/s². How far does the car travel before stopping?

Solution:

  1. Identify what we know:
    • v₀ = 30 m/s (initial velocity)
    • v = 0 m/s (final velocity - the car stops)
    • a = -8 m/s² (negative because it's slowing down)
    • x - x₀ = ? (displacement - what we're solving for)
  2. Choose the right equation:

    We don't know time, so use: v² = v₀² + 2a(x - x₀)

  3. Substitute and solve:

    0² = 30² + 2(-8)(x - x₀)

    0 = 900 - 16(x - x₀)

    16(x - x₀) = 900

    x - x₀ = 56.25 m

Answer: 56.25 meters

Real-world insight: This is why highway safety guidelines recommend maintaining a following distance of at least 2 seconds. At 30 m/s, you'd travel 60 m in 2 seconds - just enough to brake safely if the car ahead stops suddenly.

Example 2: Drag Racing

Problem: A drag racer accelerates from rest at 12 m/s² for 5 seconds. What is the racer's final velocity, and how far did they travel?

Solution:

Part A: Final velocity

  1. Known: v₀ = 0 m/s, a = 12 m/s², t = 5 s
  2. Use: v = v₀ + at
  3. v = 0 + (12)(5) = 60 m/s

Part B: Distance traveled

  1. Known: v₀ = 0 m/s, a = 12 m/s², t = 5 s, x₀ = 0
  2. Use: x = x₀ + v₀t + ½at²
  3. x = 0 + 0(5) + ½(12)(5²)
  4. x = ½(12)(25) = 150 m

Final velocity: 60 m/s (216 km/h)

Distance: 150 meters

Real-world insight: Professional drag racers reach speeds over 530 km/h in just 4 seconds, covering about 300 meters. The acceleration forces are so intense (5g+) that drivers need specialized safety equipment.

Quick Check: Test Your Understanding

Question: A ball is thrown upward with an initial velocity of 20 m/s. If gravity causes a downward acceleration of -10 m/s², how long until the ball reaches its maximum height? (Hint: At maximum height, velocity = 0)

Answer: 2 seconds

Solution:

Use v = v₀ + at

0 = 20 + (-10)t

10t = 20

t = 2 s

At the peak, the ball momentarily stops (v = 0) before falling back down.

Practice Questions

Question 1 (Easy): What does the slope of a position-time graph represent?

A) Acceleration
B) Velocity
C) Displacement
D) Force

Answer: B) Velocity

The slope (rise/run) of a position-time graph is Δx/Δt, which is the definition of velocity.

Question 2 (Medium): A train accelerates from 10 m/s to 30 m/s in 8 seconds. What is its acceleration?

Answer: 2.5 m/s²

Solution:

a = Δv / Δt = (30 - 10) / 8 = 20 / 8 = 2.5 m/s²

Question 3 (Medium): A cyclist accelerates from rest at 2 m/s² for 6 seconds. How far do they travel?

Answer: 36 meters

Solution:

x = v₀t + ½at²

x = 0(6) + ½(2)(6²)

x = 0 + ½(2)(36)

x = 36 m

Question 4 (Hard): A car traveling at 25 m/s needs to stop within 40 meters. What minimum deceleration is required?

Answer: -7.8 m/s²

Solution:

Use v² = v₀² + 2a(x - x₀)

0² = 25² + 2a(40)

0 = 625 + 80a

a = -625/80 = -7.8 m/s²

Negative indicates deceleration (slowing down).

Question 5 (Hard): A rocket accelerates upward from rest at 15 m/s² for 4 seconds, then the engine cuts off. How high does the rocket go? (Assume gravity = -10 m/s²)

Answer: 300 meters

Solution (two phases):

Phase 1 (engine on):

x₁ = ½(15)(4²) = 120 m

v = 0 + 15(4) = 60 m/s (velocity when engine cuts)

Phase 2 (coasting up):

v² = v₀² + 2a(x - x₀)

0² = 60² + 2(-10)(x₂)

x₂ = 3600/20 = 180 m

Total: 120 + 180 = 300 m

Quick Summary

  • Kinematics describes motion using position, velocity, and acceleration
  • Position-time graphs: slope = velocity; velocity-time graphs: slope = acceleration, area = displacement
  • Four kinematic equations let us solve for any variable when acceleration is constant
  • Always check your signs: positive = forward/up, negative = backward/down
  • Real applications: crash investigation, self-driving cars, space missions, sports analytics

Real-World Connection: Autonomous Vehicles

Self-driving cars use kinematics constantly:

  • Braking Distance: Calculate stopping distance based on current speed and road conditions
  • Collision Avoidance: Predict whether another car will enter your path
  • Smooth Acceleration: Optimize passenger comfort by controlling acceleration rates
  • Merge Planning: Time acceleration to match highway traffic speed

A Tesla's Autopilot system makes these kinematic calculations thousands of times per second, using the same physics you just learned. The difference? You're solving one problem at a time; the car's computer is solving them for every object around you simultaneously.

Challenge Problem

The Safe Following Distance Problem:

Two cars are traveling at 25 m/s with 30 meters between them. The lead car suddenly brakes with deceleration of -6 m/s². The driver of the following car has a reaction time of 0.8 seconds before applying brakes with the same deceleration.

Will the cars collide? If so, at what speed? If not, what is the minimum separation distance?

Hint: Break the problem into three phases:

1. Lead car braking (0 to t seconds)

2. Following car reaction time (0 to 0.8 s) - still moving at constant velocity

3. Following car braking (0.8 s to t seconds)

Find when both cars have the same velocity, then compare positions.

Step 1: How long until lead car stops?

v = v₀ + at → 0 = 25 + (-6)t → t = 4.17 s

Step 2: Lead car position when stopped

x = v₀t + ½at² = 25(4.17) + ½(-6)(4.17²) = 52.1 m

Step 3: Following car during reaction time (0-0.8s)

Distance traveled: 25(0.8) = 20 m

Position: 30 + 20 = 50 m behind start (gap now 52.1 - 50 = 2.1 m)

Step 4: Following car braking (needs to stop in 2.1 m)

Braking distance from 25 m/s: v² = v₀² + 2ax

0 = 625 + 2(-6)x → x = 52.1 m

Conclusion:

COLLISION! The following car needs 52.1 m to stop but only has 2.1 m.

Impact velocity:

v² = 25² + 2(-6)(2.1) = 625 - 25.2 = 599.8

v = 24.5 m/s (88 km/h) - a severe crash!

Lesson: The "2-second rule" exists because human reaction time + braking distance require substantial following distance. At 25 m/s, a 2-second gap would be 50 meters - enough to avoid this collision.
