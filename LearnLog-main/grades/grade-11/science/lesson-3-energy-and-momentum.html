Two cars collide head-on at an intersection. Car A is 1,200 kg going 20 m/s. Car B is 1,800 kg going 15 m/s. After the crash, they stick together. How fast are they moving? Conservation of momentum gives us the exact answer - the same physics that protects you in a collision.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Apply conservation of energy to solve mechanical problems
  • Calculate kinetic and potential energy in various scenarios
  • Use conservation of momentum to analyze collisions
  • Distinguish between elastic and inelastic collisions

What You'll Learn

Energy and momentum are conserved quantities - they can't be created or destroyed, only transferred or transformed. These conservation laws are among the most powerful tools in physics, allowing us to solve complex problems without tracking every force.

From billiard balls to car crashes to rocket launches, energy and momentum conservation explain it all.

Why This Matters

Conservation laws drive technology and safety:

  • Car Safety: Crumple zones absorb kinetic energy to protect passengers
  • Sports: Baseball bats, golf clubs, and tennis rackets optimized for energy transfer
  • Renewable Energy: Converting wind/water kinetic energy to electrical energy
  • Space Exploration: Momentum conservation enables rocket propulsion
  • Amusement Rides: Energy transformations between potential and kinetic create thrills

Core Concept: Energy

Kinetic Energy (KE)

Energy of motion.

Formula: KE = ½mv²

Example: A 1,000 kg car at 20 m/s has KE = ½(1000)(20²) = 200,000 J

Potential Energy (PE)

Stored energy due to position or configuration.

Gravitational PE: PE = mgh (mass × gravity × height)

Example: A 50 kg person 10 m high has PE = (50)(9.8)(10) = 4,900 J

Conservation of Mechanical Energy

In the absence of friction and air resistance:

Formula: KE₁ + PE₁ = KE₂ + PE₂

Total mechanical energy remains constant - energy transforms between kinetic and potential but the sum never changes.

Visual: Energy Transformation on a Roller Coaster

Key insight: Total energy (KE + PE) stays constant. As the car descends, PE converts to KE. As it climbs, KE converts back to PE.

Core Concept: Momentum

Momentum (p)

The quantity of motion an object has.

Formula: p = mv (mass × velocity)

Example: A 1,500 kg car at 20 m/s has p = (1500)(20) = 30,000 kg·m/s

Conservation of Momentum

In a closed system (no external forces), total momentum before = total momentum after.

Formula: Σp_before = Σp_after

This is especially powerful for analyzing collisions.

Impulse (J)

Change in momentum, equal to force applied over time.

Formula: J = Δp = FΔt

Same momentum change can result from large force over short time OR small force over long time.

Types of Collisions

Elastic Collision

Both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved.

  • Objects bounce off each other
  • Example: Billiard balls, atomic particles
  • No energy lost to deformation or heat

Inelastic Collision

Momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is NOT conserved (some converts to heat, sound, deformation).

  • Objects may stick together (perfectly inelastic)
  • Example: Car crashes, clay balls colliding
  • Energy "lost" is actually transformed to other forms

Example 1: Pendulum Energy Conservation

Problem: A 2 kg pendulum is released from a height of 1.5 m. What is its maximum speed at the lowest point? (Ignore air resistance)

Solution:

  1. Identify energy at release point (top):
    • PE = mgh = (2)(9.8)(1.5) = 29.4 J
    • KE = 0 (released from rest)
    • Total energy = 29.4 J
  2. Energy at lowest point (bottom):
    • PE = 0 (h = 0)
    • KE = ? (all energy is now kinetic)
    • Total energy = 29.4 J (same as before)
  3. Solve for velocity:

    KE = ½mv²

    29.4 = ½(2)v²

    29.4 = v²

    v = 5.4 m/s

Answer: 5.4 m/s

Real-world insight: Pendulum clocks use this energy transformation. The pendulum swings with constant total energy, making it a reliable timekeeper.

Example 2: Car Collision (Inelastic)

Problem: Car A (1,200 kg) traveling east at 20 m/s collides with Car B (1,800 kg) traveling west at 15 m/s. They stick together after the collision. What is their combined velocity after impact?

Solution:

  1. Define positive direction (east = +):
    • Car A: m₁ = 1,200 kg, v₁ = +20 m/s
    • Car B: m₂ = 1,800 kg, v₂ = -15 m/s (west is negative)
  2. Calculate total momentum before:

    p_before = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂

    p_before = (1200)(20) + (1800)(-15)

    p_before = 24,000 - 27,000 = -3,000 kg·m/s

  3. Apply conservation of momentum:

    p_after = p_before

    (m₁ + m₂)v_final = -3,000

    (3,000)v_final = -3,000

    v_final = -1 m/s

Answer: 1 m/s west (negative indicates westward)

Real-world insight: The heavier car (B) had more momentum, so the combined wreckage moves in Car B's original direction. This is why head-on collisions with trucks are so dangerous - the truck's momentum dominates.

Quick Check: Test Your Understanding

Question: A 0.5 kg ball is dropped from 5 m. What is its kinetic energy just before hitting the ground?

Answer: 24.5 J

Solution:

At top: PE = mgh = (0.5)(9.8)(5) = 24.5 J, KE = 0

At bottom: PE = 0, KE = ?

Energy conservation: KE_bottom = PE_top = 24.5 J

Practice Questions

Question 1 (Easy): What is the kinetic energy of a 10 kg object moving at 5 m/s?

Answer: 125 J

KE = ½mv² = ½(10)(5²) = ½(10)(25) = 125 J

Question 2 (Medium): A 60 kg person climbs 10 m up a ladder. How much potential energy did they gain?

Answer: 5,880 J

PE = mgh = (60)(9.8)(10) = 5,880 J

Question 3 (Medium): A 1,000 kg car traveling at 15 m/s. What is its momentum?

Answer: 15,000 kg·m/s

p = mv = (1000)(15) = 15,000 kg·m/s

Question 4 (Hard): A 3 kg ball moving at 8 m/s collides with a stationary 5 kg ball. If they stick together, what is their combined velocity?

Answer: 3 m/s

Solution:

p_before = (3)(8) + (5)(0) = 24 kg·m/s

p_after = (3 + 5)v = 8v

24 = 8v → v = 3 m/s

Question 5 (Hard): A roller coaster car starts from rest at 40 m height. What is its speed at 10 m height? (Ignore friction)

Answer: 24.2 m/s

Solution:

Energy at 40 m: E = mgh = mg(40)

Energy at 10 m: E = mgh + ½mv² = mg(10) + ½mv²

Conservation: mg(40) = mg(10) + ½mv²

Cancel m: g(30) = ½v²

v² = 2g(30) = 2(9.8)(30) = 588

v = 24.2 m/s

Quick Summary

  • Energy is the capacity to do work; it can transform but total is conserved
  • KE = ½mv² (energy of motion); PE = mgh (gravitational potential energy)
  • Momentum p = mv; conserved in collisions
  • Elastic collisions: KE and momentum conserved
  • Inelastic collisions: Only momentum conserved (KE transforms to heat/deformation)
  • Impulse J = FΔt = Δp: Extending collision time reduces peak force

Real-World Connection: Baseball Physics

When a bat hits a baseball, energy and momentum are both at play:

  • Momentum Transfer: The bat (heavier, slower) transfers momentum to the ball (lighter, faster)
  • Energy Transfer: Not all bat KE transfers to the ball - some becomes sound (crack!), vibration (sting in hands), and heat
  • Sweet Spot: Hitting the bat's sweet spot maximizes energy transfer and minimizes vibration, launching the ball farther
  • Exit Velocity: MLB home runs require exit velocities of 95+ mph, achieved through optimal energy and momentum transfer

A 0.145 kg baseball hit by a 0.9 kg bat (swing speed 80 mph) can exit at 110 mph. The collision lasts about 1 millisecond, with peak forces exceeding 8,000 N - enough to compress the ball to half its diameter!

Challenge Problem

The Ballistic Pendulum:

A 10 g bullet is fired into a 2 kg wooden block suspended by strings (a ballistic pendulum). The bullet embeds in the block, and they swing upward together to a maximum height of 0.5 m. What was the bullet's initial speed?

Solve this two-step problem using both momentum and energy conservation.

Hint: This is a two-phase problem:

Phase 1 (collision): Use momentum conservation to find velocity just after impact

Phase 2 (swing): Use energy conservation to relate velocity after impact to max height

Work backward from Phase 2 to find the velocity after collision, then use that in Phase 1 to find bullet speed.

Phase 2: Energy conservation during swing

At bottom (just after collision): KE = ½(m_bullet + m_block)v²

At top (max height): PE = (m_bullet + m_block)gh

Conservation: ½v² = gh

v² = 2gh = 2(9.8)(0.5) = 9.8

v = 3.13 m/s (velocity of block+bullet just after collision)

Phase 1: Momentum conservation during collision

Before: p = m_bullet × v_bullet (block at rest)

After: p = (m_bullet + m_block) × v

m_bullet × v_bullet = (m_bullet + m_block) × v

Convert: m_bullet = 0.01 kg, m_block = 2 kg

(0.01)v_bullet = (0.01 + 2)(3.13)

(0.01)v_bullet = (2.01)(3.13) = 6.29

v_bullet = 629 m/s

Answer: 629 m/s (about 1,400 mph!)

Real-world: This is how ballistic pendulums historically measured bullet velocities before modern chronographs. The tiny bullet transfers its momentum to the massive block, allowing us to measure the much slower combined velocity and work backward.
