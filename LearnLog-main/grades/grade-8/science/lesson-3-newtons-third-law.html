Newton's Third Law: Action-Reaction

Grade 8 | Science | Unit 1: Forces and Motion

🏠 Home 📚 Grade 8 🔬 Science

🚀 How does a rocket move in space where there's nothing to push against? Why do you move backward when you jump off a skateboard? The answer: every force has an equal and opposite partner - Newton's Third Law!

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • State and explain Newton's Third Law of Motion
  • Identify action-reaction force pairs in real situations
  • Understand that action and reaction forces act on different objects
  • Apply the Third Law to explain rocket propulsion, swimming, and walking

📚 What You'll Learn

Newton's Third Law states: "For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

More precisely: When object A exerts a force on object B, object B exerts an equal and opposite force back on object A.

KEY: The forces are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on DIFFERENT objects!

🌟 Why This Matters

The Third Law explains how we move and interact with the world:

  • Walking & Running: You push Earth backward, Earth pushes you forward
  • Swimming: You push water backward, water pushes you forward
  • Rocket Science: Rocket pushes gas backward, gas pushes rocket forward
  • Sports: Athletes use reaction forces to jump, accelerate, and change direction
  • Flight: Planes push air down (with wings), air pushes plane up (lift)

💡 Core Concept: Action-Reaction Pairs

Understanding Force Pairs

Forces ALWAYS come in pairs. You can never have a single isolated force!

Key Properties of Action-Reaction Pairs:
  • Equal magnitude: If action = 50 N, reaction = 50 N
  • Opposite direction: If action points right, reaction points left
  • Same type of force: Both are contact forces or both are field forces
  • Different objects: Action on object A, reaction on object B
  • Simultaneous: They happen at the exact same time

Common Misconception

❌ Wrong: "Action and reaction cancel out, so nothing moves."

✅ Correct: Action and reaction act on DIFFERENT objects, so they don't cancel! Each object experiences only one of the forces.

Example: Pushing a wall
  • Action: You push wall with 100 N → (acts on wall)
  • Reaction: Wall pushes you with 100 N ← (acts on you)
  • Result: Wall doesn't move (too massive), but you slide backward!

The forces don't cancel because they act on different objects!

🎨 Visual: Action-Reaction Force Pairs

✏️ Example 1: Identifying Force Pairs

Scenario: A tennis player hits a ball with their racket. Identify the action-reaction force pair.

Analysis:

  1. Action force: Racket pushes ball forward (force on ball)
  2. Reaction force: Ball pushes racket backward (force on racket)

Checking our answer:

  • ✅ Equal magnitude? Yes - same strength
  • ✅ Opposite direction? Yes - forward vs backward
  • ✅ Different objects? Yes - one on ball, one on racket
  • ✅ Simultaneous? Yes - happen at moment of contact

💡 Why you feel it: When you hit a ball hard, you feel the racket vibrate and push back on your hand. That's the reaction force! The harder you hit (action), the more you feel the impact (reaction).

✏️ Example 2: Why Rockets Work in Space

Problem: Some people think rockets can't work in space because "there's nothing to push against." Use Newton's Third Law to explain why they're wrong.

Solution:

  1. The misconception: Rockets need to push against air or ground
  2. The truth: Rockets push against their own exhaust!
  3. Action-reaction pair:
    • Action: Rocket pushes exhaust gas backward at high speed
    • Reaction: Exhaust gas pushes rocket forward with equal force
  4. Why space is actually better:
    • No air resistance to slow the rocket down
    • No friction with ground
    • The rocket carries everything it needs (fuel + oxidizer)

The rocket doesn't push on space - it pushes on its own exhaust!

🎮 Quick Check: Test Your Understanding!

Question: You're in a canoe in the middle of a calm lake. You throw your backpack toward the shore. What happens to the canoe?

Answer: The canoe moves backward (away from shore)!

Explanation using Newton's Third Law:

  • Action: You push backpack toward shore
  • Reaction: Backpack pushes you (and canoe) backward

The harder you throw the backpack, the faster the canoe moves backward. This is how astronauts maneuver in space - they throw objects or use gas jets!

📝 Practice Questions

Question 1 (Easy): When you push on a door, what pushes back on you?

A) Air pressure
B) The door
C) Gravity
D) Your muscles

Answer: B) The door

By Newton's Third Law, when you push the door, the door pushes you with equal force in the opposite direction. That's why you can feel resistance!

Question 2 (Medium): A book sits on a table. Identify TWO separate action-reaction pairs in this situation.

Answer:

Pair 1 (Gravity):

  • Action: Earth pulls book down
  • Reaction: Book pulls Earth up (we don't notice because Earth is so massive!)

Pair 2 (Contact):

  • Action: Book pushes down on table
  • Reaction: Table pushes up on book (this is the normal force)

Question 3 (Medium): Why can't you move a car by sitting inside and pushing on the dashboard?

Answer: The action-reaction forces are both acting on parts of the same system (you + car).

Explanation:

  • Action: You push dashboard forward
  • Reaction: Dashboard pushes you backward
  • Result: You move backward in the seat, car doesn't move

To move the car, you need an external force - like pushing against the ground outside. That's why you can't lift yourself by pulling on your own shoelaces!

Question 4 (Hard): A 60 kg astronaut throws a 2 kg wrench at 10 m/s in space. Using Newton's Third Law, calculate how fast the astronaut moves backward. (Hint: the forces are equal, but masses are different!)

Answer: 0.33 m/s backward (about 1/3 m/s)

Solution:

Newton's Third Law says forces are equal:

  • Force on wrench = Force on astronaut
  • Using F = ma: m₁a₁ = m₂a₂
  • 2 kg × 10 m/s² = 60 kg × a₂
  • 20 = 60a₂
  • a₂ = 0.33 m/s²

Since acceleration happens over the same time, velocity ratio equals mass ratio (inverted): v₂ = 2/60 × 10 = 0.33 m/s

Question 5 (Hard): Explain why a lighter person on ice skates can push a heavier person and both people move, but the lighter person moves faster backward. Which law(s) are involved?

Answer: Both Newton's Second and Third Laws!

Third Law: When light person pushes heavy person, they exert equal and opposite forces on each other (say 100 N each way).

Second Law: a = F/m, so same force creates different accelerations!

  • Light person (40 kg): a = 100 N ÷ 40 kg = 2.5 m/s²
  • Heavy person (80 kg): a = 100 N ÷ 80 kg = 1.25 m/s²

Result: Light person accelerates backward twice as fast! This is why smaller hockey players get checked harder when they collide with bigger players.

📌 Quick Summary

  • Newton's Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction
  • Force pairs are: Equal magnitude, opposite direction, different objects
  • They don't cancel because they act on different objects
  • Examples: Walking (push ground, ground pushes you), swimming, rockets, jumping
  • Can't have a single force - forces always come in pairs!
  • Works in space - rockets push exhaust, exhaust pushes rocket

🌍 Real-World Connection: Swimming Faster

Olympic swimmers use Newton's Third Law to maximize speed:

🏊 Technique Breakdown:

  • Hand position: Cupped hands and angled arms push more water = bigger action force = bigger reaction force = faster swimming!
  • Streamlining: Reduce water resistance so the reaction force accelerates you more efficiently
  • Leg kicks: Push water down and back, water pushes you up and forward
  • Wall push: Explosive push off wall at turns creates huge reaction force for acceleration

The physics advantage: Elite swimmers understand that to go faster forward, they need to push more water backward with more force. It's not about fighting the water - it's about using it! The same principle applies to rowing, kayaking, and sailing.

Fun fact: Michael Phelps' technique pushed so much water per stroke that his reaction forces propelled him faster than competitors with higher stroke rates!

🚀 Challenge Problem

The Ice Skating Problem:

Two ice skaters, Alex (50 kg) and Sam (100 kg), face each other and push off. After pushing:

  • Alex slides backward at 4 m/s
  • Sam slides backward at unknown velocity

Part A: Explain why both skaters move backward using Newton's Third Law.

Part B: Calculate Sam's velocity after the push. (Hint: the forces are equal, but masses and accelerations differ!)

Part C: If they push harder (doubling the force), what happens to their velocities?

Hints:

  • Part A: What does each skater push on?
  • Part B: Equal forces on different masses... think momentum or F = ma!
  • Part C: If force doubles for both, what happens to acceleration?

Part A Answer:

Alex pushes on Sam (action force), and Sam pushes on Alex (reaction force). These forces are equal and opposite by Newton's Third Law, so both skaters accelerate backward.

Part B Answer: Sam moves at 2 m/s

Solution using conservation of momentum:

Since forces are equal and opposite, the momenta must be equal and opposite:

m₁v₁ = m₂v₂

50 kg × 4 m/s = 100 kg × v₂

200 = 100v₂

v₂ = 2 m/s

Key insight: Sam moves half as fast because they have twice the mass. Equal force, but different acceleration (a = F/m)!

Part C Answer: Both velocities double!

Explanation:

  • Double the force → double the acceleration (F = ma)
  • Same time interval → double the final velocity
  • Alex: 4 m/s → 8 m/s
  • Sam: 2 m/s → 4 m/s
  • The velocity ratio stays the same (2:1) because mass ratio doesn't change!

This is why in ice hockey, smaller players get knocked back harder in collisions - same force, less mass, more acceleration! 🏒
