Newton's Second Law: F = ma

Grade 8 | Science | Unit 1: Forces and Motion

🏠 Home 📚 Grade 8 🔬 Science

🏎️ Why can a tiny electric motor accelerate a toy car instantly, but a massive truck needs a huge engine to speed up? Why do sports cars accelerate faster than buses? The secret is in the most famous physics equation: F = ma!

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • State and explain Newton's Second Law of Motion
  • Use the formula F = ma to calculate force, mass, or acceleration
  • Understand the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration
  • Apply the Second Law to real-world scenarios like vehicles and sports

📚 What You'll Learn

Newton's Second Law states: "The acceleration of an object depends on the net force acting on it and its mass."

This is expressed mathematically as:

F = ma

F = Force (in Newtons, N)
m = mass (in kilograms, kg)
a = acceleration (in meters per second squared, m/s²)

🌟 Why This Matters

The Second Law is everywhere in engineering and sports:

  • Vehicle Design: Engineers calculate engine power needed for desired acceleration
  • Sports Performance: Athletes optimize force application for maximum acceleration
  • Rocket Science: NASA calculates thrust needed to accelerate spacecraft
  • Video Games: Physics engines use F = ma for realistic vehicle handling
  • Safety Engineering: Crash test calculations to design safer cars

💡 Core Concept: Understanding F = ma

Breaking Down the Formula

Force (F):
  • A push or pull on an object
  • Measured in Newtons (N)
  • 1 N = force needed to accelerate 1 kg at 1 m/s²
Mass (m):
  • Amount of matter in an object
  • Measured in kilograms (kg)
  • More mass = more inertia = harder to accelerate
Acceleration (a):
  • Rate of change in velocity
  • Measured in meters per second squared (m/s²)
  • Can be speeding up, slowing down, or changing direction

Key Relationships

1. More force → More acceleration (if mass stays the same)

Push a skateboard harder → it accelerates faster

2. More mass → Less acceleration (if force stays the same)

Same push on a loaded truck → accelerates slower than empty truck

3. Rearranging the formula:

  • F = ma (find force)
  • a = F/m (find acceleration)
  • m = F/a (find mass)

✏️ Example 1: Calculating Force

Problem: A 1200 kg car accelerates from rest to highway speed at 3 m/s². What force does the engine apply?

Solution:

  1. Identify what we know:
    • Mass (m) = 1200 kg
    • Acceleration (a) = 3 m/s²
    • Force (F) = ?
  2. Choose the right formula: F = ma
  3. Substitute and calculate:

    F = m × a

    F = 1200 kg × 3 m/s²

    F = 3600 N

Answer: 3600 N (Newtons)

💡 Real-world context: That's about 810 pounds of force! This is why cars need powerful engines - they need to generate huge forces to accelerate their massive weight.

✏️ Example 2: Calculating Acceleration

Problem: A soccer player kicks a 0.4 kg ball with a force of 120 N. What is the ball's acceleration?

Solution:

  1. Identify what we know:
    • Force (F) = 120 N
    • Mass (m) = 0.4 kg
    • Acceleration (a) = ?
  2. Rearrange formula to solve for a: a = F/m
  3. Substitute and calculate:

    a = F ÷ m

    a = 120 N ÷ 0.4 kg

    a = 300 m/s²

Answer: 300 m/s²

💡 Why so high? The ball has very little mass! The same force that barely budges a person creates massive acceleration on a light soccer ball. This is why professional kicks can launch balls over 100 km/h!

🎮 Quick Check: Test Your Understanding!

Question: Two shopping carts are pushed with the same force. Cart A is empty (10 kg), Cart B is full of groceries (40 kg). Which accelerates faster?

Answer: Cart A (empty cart) accelerates 4 times faster!

Calculation:

Using a = F/m, if both receive force F:

  • Cart A: a = F/10 (faster)
  • Cart B: a = F/40 (slower)

Cart B has 4× the mass, so it has 1/4 the acceleration. This is why grocery stores have heavy carts that are hard to accelerate!

📝 Practice Questions

Question 1 (Easy): If you double the force on an object while keeping mass constant, what happens to acceleration?

A) Stays the same
B) Doubles
C) Halves
D) Quadruples

Answer: B) Doubles

From F = ma, we get a = F/m. If F doubles and m stays the same, acceleration must double. Force and acceleration are directly proportional!

Question 2 (Medium): A 60 kg skateboarder accelerates at 2 m/s². Calculate the net force acting on them.

Answer: 120 N

Solution:

F = ma

F = 60 kg × 2 m/s²

F = 120 N

Question 3 (Medium): A rocket with a mass of 5000 kg experiences a thrust force of 75,000 N. What is its acceleration?

Answer: 15 m/s²

Solution:

a = F/m

a = 75,000 N ÷ 5000 kg

a = 15 m/s²

That's 1.5 times Earth's gravity - intense acceleration!

Question 4 (Hard): A 800 kg elevator accelerates upward at 1.5 m/s². The cable must support both the elevator's weight AND provide the upward force. What total force must the cable exert? (Hint: gravity = 10 m/s² downward)

Answer: 9200 N

Solution:

1. Force to accelerate upward: F = ma = 800 kg × 1.5 m/s² = 1200 N

2. Force to counteract gravity: F = ma = 800 kg × 10 m/s² = 8000 N

3. Total cable force = 1200 N + 8000 N = 9200 N

The cable must overcome gravity PLUS provide extra force for acceleration!

Question 5 (Hard): A 2 kg drone can produce a maximum thrust of 30 N. On Earth, gravity pulls it down with force of 20 N (2 kg × 10 m/s²). What is the maximum upward acceleration the drone can achieve?

Answer: 5 m/s² upward

Solution:

1. Net force = Thrust - Weight = 30 N - 20 N = 10 N upward

2. Acceleration = F/m = 10 N ÷ 2 kg = 5 m/s² upward

Key insight: Some thrust is "wasted" fighting gravity. Only the leftover force (10 N) accelerates the drone upward!

📌 Quick Summary

  • Newton's Second Law: F = ma (Force = mass × acceleration)
  • Force (F): Measured in Newtons (N)
  • Mass (m): Measured in kilograms (kg)
  • Acceleration (a): Measured in m/s²
  • More force → More acceleration (same mass)
  • More mass → Less acceleration (same force)
  • Rearrange for any variable: a = F/m or m = F/a

🌍 Real-World Connection: Sports Cars vs. Trucks

Ever wonder why sports cars can accelerate 0-100 km/h in 3 seconds, but a loaded truck takes 30 seconds? It's all F = ma!

🏎️ Sports Car Example:

  • Mass: 1500 kg (lightweight design)
  • Engine force: 6000 N
  • Acceleration: a = 6000/1500 = 4 m/s²

🚛 Loaded Truck Example:

  • Mass: 15,000 kg (10× heavier!)
  • Engine force: 6000 N (same as car)
  • Acceleration: a = 6000/15,000 = 0.4 m/s²

Result: The sports car accelerates 10× faster! Even with the same engine force, the massive truck has so much inertia that it accelerates slowly. Car manufacturers understand this - they either reduce mass (lightweight materials) or increase force (bigger engines) to improve acceleration!

🚀 Challenge Problem

The Rocket Launch Problem:

SpaceX's Falcon 9 rocket has these specs:

  • Mass at launch: 550,000 kg (including fuel)
  • Thrust from engines: 7,600,000 N
  • Gravity pulls down with: 5,500,000 N (550,000 kg × 10 m/s²)

Part A: What is the net upward force after accounting for gravity?

Part B: What is the rocket's initial acceleration?

Part C: As fuel burns, the rocket's mass decreases to 100,000 kg, but thrust stays at 7,600,000 N. What's the new acceleration? Why is this important?

Hints:

  • Part A: Net force = Thrust - Gravity force
  • Part B: Use a = F/m with the NET force
  • Part C: Recalculate with new mass. Don't forget gravity changes too!

Part A Answer: 2,100,000 N upward

Calculation:

Net Force = Thrust - Gravity

= 7,600,000 N - 5,500,000 N

= 2,100,000 N upward

Part B Answer: 3.8 m/s²

Calculation:

a = F/m

a = 2,100,000 N ÷ 550,000 kg

a = 3.8 m/s²

Part C Answer: 66 m/s² (massive increase!)

Calculation:

New gravity force = 100,000 kg × 10 m/s² = 1,000,000 N

Net force = 7,600,000 N - 1,000,000 N = 6,600,000 N

a = 6,600,000 N ÷ 100,000 kg = 66 m/s²

🚀 Why this matters: The rocket accelerates 17× faster near the end! This is why rockets start slow and end up screaming fast - as fuel burns away, mass decreases dramatically, so the same thrust produces way more acceleration. This is critical for reaching orbital velocity!
