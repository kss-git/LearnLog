🏹 Imagine drawing back a bow and arrow. The arrow isn't moving yet, but you can feel the tension in the string. That stored energy waiting to be released? 🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

📚 What You'll Learn

Potential energy is stored energy - energy that an object has because of its position, condition, or chemical composition. It's energy waiting to be released and transformed into other forms.

The most common type is gravitational potential energy - the energy stored in objects that are above the ground. The higher and heavier an object is, the more potential energy it has. When released, this energy converts to kinetic energy!

PE = mgh

where m = mass (kg), g = gravity (9.8 m/s²), h = height (m)

🌟 Why This Matters

Potential energy is fundamental to how we store and use energy:

  • Hydroelectric Dams: Water stored at height has potential energy that generates 16% of the world's electricity
  • Batteries: Chemical potential energy powers everything from phones to electric cars
  • Roller Coasters: The initial climb stores potential energy for the entire ride
  • Food: The chemical potential energy in food fuels your body
  • Pumped Storage: Massive "water batteries" that store energy by pumping water uphill

Without understanding potential energy, we couldn't design energy storage systems, create thrilling amusement rides, or even understand how our bodies work!

💡 Core Concept: Types of Potential Energy

1. Gravitational Potential Energy

Energy stored due to an object's height above the ground. The higher it is, the more potential energy it has.

Formula: PE = mgh
  • PE = Potential Energy in Joules (J)
  • m = mass in kilograms (kg)
  • g = acceleration due to gravity = 9.8 m/s² (or ~10 m/s² for estimates)
  • h = height above ground in meters (m)

Example: A book on a shelf, water at the top of a waterfall, a diver on a platform

2. Elastic Potential Energy

Energy stored in stretched or compressed objects that can return to their original shape.

Examples: Stretched rubber bands, compressed springs, drawn bow and arrow, trampoline

3. Chemical Potential Energy

Energy stored in the bonds between atoms and molecules.

Examples: Food, batteries, gasoline, natural gas, wood

Why Height Matters

In the formula PE = mgh, height (h) is directly proportional to potential energy. Double the height, and you double the potential energy. This is why rollercoasters start with a tall climb - they're storing up energy for the rest of the ride!

🎨 Visual: Potential Energy at Different Heights

All three balls have the same mass (1 kg), but their potential energy increases with height. The highest ball has over 3× the energy of the lowest ball! When dropped, this potential energy will convert to kinetic energy.

✏️ Example 1: Calculating Potential Energy

Problem: A 60 kg diver stands on a platform 10 meters above the water. Calculate the gravitational potential energy stored. (Use g = 10 m/s² for simplicity)

Solution:

  1. Write the formula: PE = mgh
  2. Identify the values:
    • m = 60 kg
    • g = 10 m/s²
    • h = 10 m
  3. Substitute and calculate:

    PE = 60 × 10 × 10

    PE = 6,000 J

Answer: 6,000 Joules (or 6 kJ)

💡 What this means: When the diver jumps, all 6,000 J of potential energy will convert to kinetic energy. Just before hitting the water, the diver will have 6,000 J of kinetic energy and be moving at about 14 m/s (50 km/h)!

✏️ Example 2: Comparing Heights

Problem: A 0.5 kg basketball is held at 2 m high. How high would you need to lift it to double its potential energy? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate original PE at h = 2 m

PE₁ = mgh = 0.5 × 10 × 2 = 10 J

Step 2: Find height for double the PE

We want PE₂ = 2 × PE₁ = 2 × 10 = 20 J

20 = 0.5 × 10 × h

20 = 5h

h = 4 m

Answer: 4 meters

To double the potential energy, you need to double the height!

💡 Key Insight: Potential energy is directly proportional to height. Double the height = double the energy. Triple the height = triple the energy. This is different from kinetic energy, where doubling speed gives you 4× the energy!

🎮 Quick Check: Test Your Understanding!

Question: Two identical books (same mass) are on different shelves. Book A is twice as high as Book B. How many times more potential energy does Book A have?

Answer: 2 times more (double)

Explanation:

Since PE = mgh and both books have the same mass, the only difference is height.

If Book A is at height 2h and Book B is at height h:

PE_A = mg(2h) = 2(mgh)

PE_B = mgh

So Book A has exactly twice the potential energy!

📝 Practice Questions

Question 1 (Easy): Which type of potential energy is stored in a stretched rubber band?

A) Gravitational
B) Elastic
C) Chemical
D) Kinetic

Answer: B) Elastic potential energy

When you stretch or compress something that can return to its original shape (like a rubber band, spring, or trampoline), it stores elastic potential energy.

Question 2 (Medium): A 2 kg textbook sits on a shelf 1.5 m above the floor. Calculate its gravitational potential energy. (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: 30 J

Solution:

PE = mgh

PE = 2 × 10 × 1.5

PE = 30 Joules

Question 3 (Medium): If you lift an object from 3 m to 9 m high, by what factor does its potential energy increase?

Answer: 3 times

Explanation:

The new height (9 m) is 3 times the original height (3 m).

Since PE is directly proportional to height, the potential energy also increases by a factor of 3.

Check: PE₁ = mg(3), PE₂ = mg(9) = 3 × mg(3) ✓

Question 4 (Hard): A 1,500 kg car is raised 2.5 m on a mechanic's lift. How much gravitational potential energy is stored? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: 37,500 J (or 37.5 kJ)

Solution:

PE = mgh

PE = 1,500 × 10 × 2.5

PE = 37,500 J

That's a lot of energy! If the lift fails, this energy converts to kinetic energy, which is why safety mechanisms are critical.

Question 5 (Hard): A 70 kg person climbs stairs, raising themselves 15 m. A 35 kg child uses an elevator to go up 30 m. Who gains more gravitational potential energy? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: They gain the same amount!

Solution:

Person: PE = 70 × 10 × 15 = 10,500 J

Child: PE = 35 × 10 × 30 = 10,500 J

Even though the person is heavier, the child goes twice as high. The person has 2× the mass but half the height, so it balances out perfectly!

This shows that both mass and height are equally important in the PE = mgh formula.

📌 Quick Summary

  • Potential energy is stored energy based on position or condition
  • Gravitational PE: PE = mgh (depends on mass, gravity, and height)
  • Elastic PE: stored in stretched or compressed objects
  • Chemical PE: stored in molecular bonds (food, batteries, fuel)
  • Height is directly proportional to gravitational PE (double height = double energy)
  • Potential energy can convert to kinetic energy when released

🌍 Real-World Connection: Pumped-Storage Hydroelectricity

Imagine a massive battery made entirely of water! That's exactly what pumped-storage power plants are - they're the world's largest energy storage systems.

How it works:

  • When electricity is cheap (like at night or when it's windy), pumps move millions of liters of water uphill to a high reservoir
  • This stores gravitational potential energy - PE = mgh, where m is the water's mass and h is the height difference
  • When electricity is needed (peak demand times), water flows back downhill through turbines, converting PE back to electrical energy
  • Efficiency: About 70-80% of energy is recovered - pretty good for such a simple system!

The Bath County Pumped Storage Station in Virginia can store 24,000 MWh of energy - enough to power 750,000 homes for an hour! All thanks to gravitational potential energy. This technology is crucial for balancing renewable energy sources like wind and solar, which don't always generate power when we need it. 💧⚡

🚀 Challenge Problem

The Roller Coaster Design Challenge:

You're designing a new roller coaster. The coaster car (including passengers) has a mass of 800 kg. You want the car to reach a maximum speed of 30 m/s at the bottom of the first drop.

Part A: How much kinetic energy will the car have at the bottom? (KE = ½mv²)

Part B: Assuming all potential energy converts to kinetic energy (ignoring friction), how high must the starting hill be? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Part C: If you add a 200 kg safety factor to the mass, how much higher must the hill be to achieve the same speed?

Hint: Energy conservation! At the top, the car has maximum PE and no KE (not moving). At the bottom, all PE has converted to KE.

So: PE_top = KE_bottom

This means: mgh = ½mv²

You can solve for h!

Part A Answer: 360,000 J (or 360 kJ)

Solution:

KE = ½mv²

KE = ½ × 800 × 30²

KE = 400 × 900

KE = 360,000 J

Part B Answer: 45 meters

Solution:

Set PE = KE:

mgh = 360,000

800 × 10 × h = 360,000

8,000h = 360,000

h = 45 m

Part C Answer: The same height - 45 meters!

Explanation:

This is the clever part! Let's use energy conservation:

mgh = ½mv²

Notice that mass (m) appears on both sides, so it cancels out:

gh = ½v²

h = v² / (2g) = 30² / (2 × 10) = 900 / 20 = 45 m

The height doesn't depend on mass! Whether the car is 800 kg or 1,000 kg, you need the same height to reach 30 m/s. Heavier cars need more PE to start, but they also need more KE to reach the same speed, so it balances perfectly. This is why all objects fall at the same rate (ignoring air resistance) - physics is beautiful! 🎢
