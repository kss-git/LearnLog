Conservation of Energy

Grade 8 | Science | Unit 2: Energy

🏠 Home 📚 Grade 8 🔬 Science

⚡ Here's one of the most powerful laws in all of physics: Energy can never be created or destroyed - it only changes forms! Every joule of energy in the universe has existed since the Big Bang and will exist forever. When you kick a soccer ball, energy doesn't appear out of nowhere - it transforms from chemical energy in your muscles to kinetic energy in the ball. This law governs everything from pendulums to planets, rollercoasters to rockets. Let's discover why energy conservation is the ultimate rule of the universe!

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • State and explain the law of conservation of energy
  • Track energy transformations in mechanical systems
  • Apply conservation of energy to solve problems (PE + KE = constant)
  • Explain where "lost" energy goes in real-world scenarios

📚 What You'll Learn

The Law of Conservation of Energy states that the total amount of energy in a closed system remains constant. Energy can transform from one type to another, but the total never changes.

In mechanical systems (like pendulums or rollercoasters), energy constantly shifts between potential and kinetic energy. When an object is high up, it has lots of PE. As it falls, PE converts to KE. The sum stays the same:

PE + KE = Total Energy (constant)

or equivalently: E_total = mgh + ½mv² = constant

🌟 Why This Matters

Conservation of energy is one of the most fundamental laws in physics:

  • Engineering: Design everything from bridges to spacecraft knowing energy must balance
  • Sports: Analyze the physics of skateboarding, basketball, and gymnastics
  • Renewable Energy: Calculate efficiency of solar panels and wind turbines
  • Space Travel: Plan orbital maneuvers and planetary slingshots
  • Climate Science: Track energy flow through Earth's atmosphere and oceans

This law is so reliable that if it appears to be violated, physicists assume we've missed something (like when neutrinos were discovered because energy seemed to disappear in radioactive decay)!

💡 Core Concept: Energy Transformations

How Energy Transforms

In a closed system (no energy entering or leaving), energy continuously changes forms but the total remains constant.

Example: A Pendulum
  • At the highest point: Maximum PE, zero KE (not moving)
  • Swinging down: PE converts to KE, total energy stays same
  • At the bottom: Zero PE (lowest point), maximum KE (fastest)
  • Swinging up: KE converts back to PE
  • Cycle repeats forever (in an ideal system with no friction)

The Conservation Equation

For mechanical systems, we can write:

PE_initial + KE_initial = PE_final + KE_final

or

mgh₁ + ½mv₁² = mgh₂ + ½mv₂²

What About "Lost" Energy?

In the real world, mechanical energy seems to decrease over time (pendulums slow down, balls stop bouncing). The energy isn't destroyed - it's transformed into:

  • Heat (thermal energy from friction)
  • Sound (vibrations in air)
  • Deformation (permanent shape changes)

The total energy is still conserved - it just spread out into forms we can't easily recover!

🎨 Visual: Energy Transformation on a Pendulum

As the pendulum swings, energy continuously transforms between PE and KE, but the total energy (100 J) never changes. At the endpoints (A and C), all energy is potential. At the bottom (B), all energy is kinetic!

✏️ Example 1: Finding Speed from Height

Problem: A 0.5 kg ball is dropped from a height of 20 m. What is its speed just before it hits the ground? (Ignore air resistance, use g = 10 m/s²)

Solution using Conservation of Energy:

  1. At the top (initial):
    • PE = mgh = 0.5 × 10 × 20 = 100 J
    • KE = 0 (not moving yet)
    • Total Energy = 100 J
  2. Just before hitting ground (final):
    • PE = 0 (h = 0)
    • KE = ? (what we're finding)
    • Total Energy must still = 100 J
  3. Apply conservation:

    PE_initial + KE_initial = PE_final + KE_final

    100 + 0 = 0 + KE_final

    KE_final = 100 J

  4. Find velocity from KE:

    ½mv² = 100

    ½(0.5)v² = 100

    0.25v² = 100

    v² = 400

    v = 20 m/s

Answer: 20 m/s

💡 Why this works: All the potential energy at the top (100 J) transformed into kinetic energy at the bottom. Notice that the mass (0.5 kg) actually cancels out - all objects fall at the same rate!

✏️ Example 2: Finding Height from Speed

Problem: A skateboard park has a halfpipe. A 60 kg skateboarder reaches a speed of 8 m/s at the bottom. How high up the other side can they coast? (Ignore friction, use g = 10 m/s²)

Solution:

Step 1: Find KE at the bottom

KE = ½mv² = ½ × 60 × 8² = 30 × 64 = 1,920 J

Step 2: At maximum height, all KE converts to PE

At highest point: velocity = 0, so KE = 0

By conservation: PE_top = KE_bottom = 1,920 J

Step 3: Find height from PE

mgh = 1,920

60 × 10 × h = 1,920

600h = 1,920

h = 3.2 m

Answer: 3.2 meters high

💡 Real-world insight: In reality, they won't quite reach 3.2 m because some energy is lost to friction and air resistance. But this gives us the theoretical maximum!

🎮 Quick Check: Test Your Understanding!

Question: A rollercoaster car has 50,000 J of total mechanical energy. At one point it has 30,000 J of kinetic energy. How much potential energy does it have at that moment?

Answer: 20,000 J

Explanation:

Total Energy = PE + KE

50,000 = PE + 30,000

PE = 50,000 - 30,000 = 20,000 J

The energy is just distributed differently - some is kinetic (motion), some is potential (height). The sum is always constant!

📝 Practice Questions

Question 1 (Easy): In a closed system, if kinetic energy increases, what must happen to potential energy?

Answer: Potential energy must decrease by the same amount

By conservation of energy, Total Energy = PE + KE = constant. If KE goes up, PE must go down so the sum stays the same.

Question 2 (Medium): A 2 kg object falls from rest at a height of 5 m. What is its kinetic energy just before hitting the ground? (g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: 100 J

Solution:

Initial PE = mgh = 2 × 10 × 5 = 100 J

Initial KE = 0 (at rest)

By conservation, all 100 J of PE converts to KE

Final KE = 100 J

Question 3 (Medium): A pendulum has 80 J of energy. At one point it has 50 J of PE. What is its KE at that point?

Answer: 30 J

Solution:

Total Energy = PE + KE

80 = 50 + KE

KE = 30 J

Question 4 (Hard): A 1.5 kg ball is thrown upward with a speed of 12 m/s. How high does it go? (g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: 7.2 meters

Solution:

Initial KE = ½mv² = ½ × 1.5 × 12² = 0.75 × 144 = 108 J

At maximum height, v = 0, so all KE becomes PE

PE = mgh = 108

1.5 × 10 × h = 108

15h = 108

h = 7.2 m

Question 5 (Hard): A 50 kg child slides down a frictionless slide from a height of 4 m. They have a speed of 2 m/s at the top. What is their speed at the bottom? (g = 10 m/s²)

Answer: 9.2 m/s (approximately)

Solution:

Top: PE₁ = 50 × 10 × 4 = 2,000 J, KE₁ = ½ × 50 × 2² = 100 J

Total Energy = 2,000 + 100 = 2,100 J

Bottom: PE₂ = 0 (h = 0), KE₂ = ?

By conservation: KE₂ = 2,100 J

½ × 50 × v² = 2,100

25v² = 2,100

v² = 84

v ≈ 9.2 m/s

The initial speed at the top adds to the final speed!

📌 Quick Summary

  • Law of Conservation of Energy: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed
  • In mechanical systems: PE + KE = constant
  • Energy transforms between forms but total energy stays the same
  • Use conservation to solve problems: E_initial = E_final
  • "Lost" energy in real systems becomes heat, sound, or deformation
  • This law applies universally - from atoms to galaxies!

🌍 Real-World Connection: Regenerative Braking

Electric cars and hybrid vehicles use a brilliant application of energy conservation called regenerative braking. Here's how it works:

Traditional Braking (Energy Wasted):

  • Car's kinetic energy → Heat in brake pads → Lost to atmosphere
  • All that energy you spent accelerating is wasted!

Regenerative Braking (Energy Conserved):

  • Car's kinetic energy → Electric motor acts as generator → Electrical energy → Stored in battery
  • When you brake, you're essentially "recharging" the battery!
  • Recovers about 70% of braking energy

The Numbers: In city driving with frequent stops, regenerative braking can extend an electric car's range by 20-30%! A Tesla Model 3 can recover enough energy from one highway exit deceleration to power the car for an extra kilometer.

This is energy conservation in action - instead of letting kinetic energy dissipate as useless heat, we transform it into useful electrical energy and store it for later. It's like getting free "refills" every time you slow down! 🔋🚗

🚀 Challenge Problem

The Bungee Jump Problem:

A 70 kg person bungee jumps from a platform 50 m above a river. The bungee cord is 30 m long when relaxed (unstretched). Use g = 10 m/s².

Part A: What is the person's gravitational PE at the platform (relative to the river)?

Part B: What is their speed after falling 30 m (when the cord just becomes taut but hasn't stretched yet)?

Part C: If they come to rest 5 m above the river, how much elastic PE is stored in the stretched cord?

Hints:

Part A: Straightforward PE = mgh calculation

Part B: After falling 30 m, height above river is 20 m. Use conservation: PE at 50m = PE at 20m + KE at 20m

Part C: At lowest point, all energy is either PE (gravitational) or elastic PE in cord. Gravitational PE at 5m + Elastic PE = Original total energy

Part A Answer: 35,000 J (or 35 kJ)

Solution:

PE = mgh = 70 × 10 × 50 = 35,000 J

Part B Answer: Approximately 24.5 m/s

Solution:

After falling 30 m, height above river = 50 - 30 = 20 m

Initial energy: PE = 35,000 J, KE = 0

At 20 m height: PE = 70 × 10 × 20 = 14,000 J

By conservation: 35,000 = 14,000 + KE

KE = 21,000 J

½mv² = 21,000

½ × 70 × v² = 21,000

35v² = 21,000

v² = 600

v ≈ 24.5 m/s (about 88 km/h - that's fast!)

Part C Answer: 31,500 J

Solution:

At rest 5 m above river:

Gravitational PE = 70 × 10 × 5 = 3,500 J

KE = 0 (at rest)

Total energy must still equal 35,000 J

Elastic PE in cord = 35,000 - 3,500 = 31,500 J

Amazing! The cord has absorbed 31.5 kJ of energy by stretching 15 m (from 30 m to 45 m). This stored elastic energy will bounce the person back up. In reality, some energy is also lost to air resistance and heat in the cord, so they won't bounce back to the full 50 m height. Safety regulations ensure the cord is strong enough to handle these forces without breaking! 🪢
