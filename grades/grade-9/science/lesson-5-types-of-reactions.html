Unit 2: Chemical Reactions

Lesson 5: Types of Chemical Reactions

Learning Objectives

  • Identify and classify five main types of chemical reactions
  • Recognize patterns in how atoms rearrange during reactions
  • Connect reaction types to real-world examples

What is a Chemical Reaction?

A chemical reaction occurs when substances interact to form new substances with different properties. The starting materials are called reactants, and the new substances formed are called products.

Five Main Types of Reactions

1. Synthesis (Combination) Reactions

Two or more simple substances combine to form a more complex product.

General Form: A + B → AB

Example: Formation of Water

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

Real-world: Hydrogen fuel cells in electric vehicles combine hydrogen and oxygen to produce water and electricity.

2. Decomposition Reactions

A complex substance breaks down into two or more simpler substances.

General Form: AB → A + B

Example: Fireworks

2KClO₃ → 2KCl + 3O₂

Real-world: Fireworks use decomposition reactions to release energy and produce colorful light displays. Potassium chlorate decomposes rapidly, releasing oxygen that helps fuel the explosion.

3. Single Displacement (Replacement) Reactions

One element replaces another element in a compound.

General Form: A + BC → AC + B

Example: Battery Chemistry

Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu

Real-world: In batteries, zinc displaces copper from copper sulfate solution, releasing electrons that flow through a circuit to power devices.

4. Double Displacement (Replacement) Reactions

Two compounds exchange partners to form two new compounds.

General Form: AB + CD → AD + CB

Example: Cooking (Making Baking Soda Volcano)

NaHCO₃ + HC₂H₃O₂ → NaC₂H₃O₂ + H₂O + CO₂

Real-world: When baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) reacts with vinegar (acetic acid), they exchange partners. This reaction produces carbon dioxide gas that creates bubbles and fizzing.

5. Combustion Reactions

A substance reacts rapidly with oxygen, releasing energy in the form of heat and light.

General Form: Fuel + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + Energy

Example: Combustion Engine

2C₈H₁₈ + 25O₂ → 16CO₂ + 18H₂O + Energy

Real-world: Gasoline (octane) combusts in car engines, producing the energy needed to power the vehicle. This is why cars need fuel and air to run!

Practice Questions

  1. Classify each reaction type:
    • CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
    • 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂
    • Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂
  2. Why do combustion engines need both fuel and air (oxygen)?
  3. Give an example of a decomposition reaction you might see in everyday life.
  4. In a battery, why does the single displacement reaction produce electricity?

Key Takeaways

  • Synthesis: Simple → Complex (A + B → AB)
  • Decomposition: Complex → Simple (AB → A + B)
  • Single Displacement: One swap (A + BC → AC + B)
  • Double Displacement: Partner swap (AB + CD → AD + CB)
  • Combustion: Fuel + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + Energy
  • These reactions power engines, create fireworks, enable cooking, and make batteries work
