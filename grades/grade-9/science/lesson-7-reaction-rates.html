Unit 2: Chemical Reactions

Lesson 7: Reaction Rates and Factors Affecting Them

Learning Objectives

  • Define reaction rate and explain how to measure it
  • Identify factors that affect how fast reactions occur
  • Apply concepts to real-world scenarios like cooking, engines, and batteries

What is Reaction Rate?

Reaction rate is the speed at which reactants are converted into products. It measures how quickly a chemical reaction occurs.

Examples of Reaction Rates

  • Very Fast: Fireworks exploding (milliseconds)
  • Fast: Combustion in a car engine (fractions of a second)
  • Medium: Cooking an egg (minutes)
  • Slow: Rusting of iron (days to years)
  • Very Slow: Formation of fossil fuels (millions of years)

Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

1. Temperature

Higher temperature = faster reaction. Heat gives particles more kinetic energy, making them move faster and collide more often.

Real-World: Cooking

Why does food cook faster at higher temperatures?

Chemical reactions that break down proteins and carbohydrates happen much faster when you increase the heat. That's why boiling water (100°C) cooks pasta in 10 minutes, but room temperature water would take forever!

  • Refrigerator (4°C): Slows down bacterial growth and food spoilage
  • Room temperature (20°C): Food reactions proceed at medium rate
  • Oven (180°C): Rapid cooking reactions - bread rises, proteins cook

2. Concentration

Higher concentration = faster reaction. More reactant particles in the same space means more collisions.

Real-World: Car Engines

Why do engines need the right fuel-air mixture?

Combustion engines mix gasoline vapor with air (oxygen). The concentration of oxygen affects how fast and efficiently the fuel burns:

  • Too little air: Incomplete combustion, wasted fuel, black smoke
  • Optimal ratio: Fast, complete combustion, maximum power
  • Too much air: Diluted mixture, slower reaction, less power

Modern engines use sensors to maintain the perfect concentration for efficient combustion!

3. Surface Area

Greater surface area = faster reaction. More exposed surface means more sites for reactions to occur.

Real-World: Cooking and Fireworks

Cooking: Why do we chop vegetables before cooking?

  • Whole potato: Takes 45 minutes to bake
  • Diced potato: Cooks in 15 minutes
  • Mashed potato: Heats in 5 minutes

More surface area means heat can reach more of the food faster!

Fireworks: Gunpowder is ground into fine powder rather than solid chunks. This massive surface area allows extremely rapid combustion - creating the spectacular explosions we see in the sky!

4. Catalysts

A catalyst is a substance that speeds up a reaction without being consumed. It provides an easier pathway for the reaction.

Real-World: Catalytic Converters in Cars

Car exhaust contains harmful pollutants like carbon monoxide (CO) and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ). The catalytic converter uses platinum and palladium as catalysts to speed up reactions that convert these into harmless CO₂ and N₂.

Without catalyst: Pollutants would react very slowly at exhaust temperatures

With catalyst: Reactions happen instantly, cleaning the exhaust before it leaves the car

Important: The catalyst isn't used up - a single catalytic converter lasts for years!

Real-World: Batteries

Battery performance depends heavily on reaction rates:

  • Cold weather: Chemical reactions slow down, reducing battery power (why phone batteries drain faster in winter)
  • Warm weather: Reactions speed up, providing more power but potentially degrading the battery faster
  • Fast charging: Uses higher voltage to speed up the charging reaction, but generates heat

Collision Theory

For a reaction to occur, particles must:

  1. Collide with each other
  2. Have enough energy (activation energy)
  3. Have the correct orientation

Practice Questions

  1. Why does milk spoil faster when left out on a hot day versus in the refrigerator?
  2. Explain why a car engine needs a catalytic converter even though the exhaust is already hot.
  3. If you want to dissolve a sugar cube quickly in water, what three things could you do? Explain why each helps.
  4. Fireworks contain powdered metals. Why is powder used instead of solid chunks?
  5. Why do battery-powered devices work more slowly in cold weather?
  6. In cooking, why does cutting food into smaller pieces reduce cooking time?
  7. Design an experiment to test how temperature affects the rate of a fizzy tablet dissolving in water.

Key Takeaways

  • Reaction rate measures how fast reactants become products
  • Temperature: Higher temperature = faster reactions (cooking, batteries)
  • Concentration: More particles = more collisions (engine fuel-air mixture)
  • Surface Area: Smaller pieces = more exposed area (chopped vegetables, firework powder)
  • Catalysts: Speed up reactions without being consumed (catalytic converters)
  • Collision theory: particles must collide with enough energy and correct orientation
  • Real applications: cooking, combustion engines, fireworks, batteries, catalytic converters
