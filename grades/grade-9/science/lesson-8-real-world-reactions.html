Unit 2: Chemical Reactions

Lesson 8: Chemical Reactions in the Real World

Learning Objectives

  • Apply chemical reaction concepts to real-world technologies
  • Understand the chemistry behind everyday devices and processes
  • Analyze the environmental and practical implications of chemical reactions

Introduction: Chemistry Powers Our World

Every day, you interact with dozens of chemical reactions without even thinking about it. From the batteries in your phone to the food you eat, chemical reactions are constantly at work. Let's explore how the principles you've learned apply to real-world technologies.

1. Combustion Engines: Chemistry in Motion

How Internal Combustion Engines Work

Cars, motorcycles, and lawn mowers use controlled explosions of fuel to create motion. This relies on combustion reactions happening thousands of times per minute!

The Chemistry of Combustion

Main Reaction (using octane from gasoline):

2C₈H₁₈ + 25O₂ → 16CO₂ + 18H₂O + Energy

Key Facts:

  • A typical car engine runs at 2,000-3,000 RPM (revolutions per minute)
  • Each piston fires about 1,000-1,500 times per minute
  • Temperature during combustion: ~2,000°C
  • One gallon of gas contains enough energy to power a car for 25-30 miles

Environmental Impact:

Burning 1 gallon of gasoline produces about 20 pounds of CO₂. For a car that drives 12,000 miles per year at 30 mpg, that's 8,000 pounds (4 tons) of CO₂ annually!

2. Batteries: Portable Chemical Reactions

How Batteries Convert Chemical Energy to Electrical Energy

Batteries use redox (reduction-oxidation) reactions to push electrons through a circuit, creating electric current.

Types of Batteries and Their Chemistry

Battery Type Chemistry Uses Key Feature
Alkaline Zn + MnO₂ TV remotes, flashlights Cheap, disposable
Lithium-Ion Li-CoO₂ Phones, laptops, EVs Rechargeable, lightweight
Lead-Acid Pb + PbO₂ + H₂SO₄ Car starters High current, heavy
Nickel-Metal Hydride NiOOH + H₂ Hybrid cars, tools Good capacity, safe

Why Li-ion Batteries Dominate:

  • High energy density (more power in less weight)
  • No "memory effect" - can be recharged anytime
  • Low self-discharge (holds charge when not in use)
  • Challenge: Can overheat if damaged (why phones have safety circuits)

3. Fireworks: Controlled Explosions

The Science of Pyrotechnics

Fireworks combine decomposition reactions, combustion, and metal ion emissions to create spectacular displays of light and sound.

Chemistry of Firework Colors

Different metal compounds produce different colors when heated:

Color Metal Compound Chemical Formula
Red Strontium compounds SrCO₃, Sr(NO₃)₂
Orange Calcium compounds CaCl₂, Ca(NO₃)₂
Yellow Sodium compounds NaNO₃, NaCl
Green Barium compounds BaCl₂, Ba(NO₃)₂
Blue Copper compounds CuCl₂, Cu(NO₃)₂
Purple Strontium + Copper mix SrCO₃ + CuCl₂
White Magnesium or Aluminum Mg, Al

Key Reactions:

Burst charge (black powder):

2KNO₃ + S + 3C → K₂S + N₂ + 3CO₂ + Energy

Color production: When metal compounds decompose from heat, electrons get excited and release specific wavelengths of light as they return to ground state.

4. Cooking: Delicious Chemistry

Chemical Reactions in Your Kitchen

Cooking is applied chemistry! Heat, acids, bases, and enzymes all trigger chemical reactions that transform raw ingredients into delicious food.

Maillard Reaction: The Science of Browning

The Maillard reaction is what gives grilled meat, toasted bread, and roasted coffee their brown color and complex flavors.

Simplified reaction:

Amino acids (from proteins) + Reducing sugars + Heat → Hundreds of flavor compounds + Brown pigments

Optimal temperature: 140-165°C (285-330°F)

Why it matters: The Maillard reaction creates over 1,000 different flavor compounds - this is why grilled steak tastes different from boiled beef, even though they're the same meat!

Baking: Rise with Chemical Leavening

Baking Soda + Acid Reaction:

NaHCO₃ + HC₂H₃O₂ → NaC₂H₃O₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑

(Baking soda + Vinegar → Sodium acetate + Water + Carbon dioxide)

Applications in baking:

  • Cakes: Baking soda reacts with acidic buttermilk or yogurt
  • Cookies: Baking powder (contains both base and acid) creates CO₂ when wetted
  • Quick breads: CO₂ bubbles get trapped in batter, making it rise

Temperature's role: Heat speeds up the reaction AND expands the CO₂ gas, making baked goods even fluffier!

Other Cooking Reactions

Process Reaction Type What Happens
Caramelization Decomposition Sugar breaks down into simpler compounds, creating brown color and nutty flavor
Egg Coagulation Denaturation Heat unfolds protein chains, which bond together making eggs solid
Pickling Acid-base Vinegar's acetic acid preserves food and adds sour flavor
Fermentation Enzymatic Yeast converts sugars to alcohol and CO₂ (bread, beer, wine)

Practice Questions

  1. Why does a car engine need both fuel AND oxygen to run? What would happen if you only had fuel?
  2. Explain why your phone battery drains faster in cold weather using reaction rate concepts.
  3. Design a firework that produces green and white colors. What metal compounds would you use?
  4. Why does meat develop a brown crust when grilled but stays gray when boiled?
  5. A baker notices their cake didn't rise. They used baking soda but forgot to add buttermilk. Explain why the cake stayed flat.
  6. Electric vehicles use lithium-ion batteries instead of gasoline. List two advantages and two disadvantages compared to combustion engines.
  7. Why is powdered metal used in fireworks instead of solid metal chunks? (Hint: think about reaction rates)
  8. Calculate: If burning 1 gallon of gas produces 20 lbs of CO₂, how much CO₂ does a car produce driving from your home to school (assume 5 miles, 30 mpg)?

Key Takeaways

  • Combustion engines: Use rapid octane combustion (C₈H₁₈ + O₂) to convert chemical energy into motion
  • Batteries: Convert chemical energy to electrical energy through redox reactions; different chemistries suit different applications
  • Fireworks: Combine decomposition (burst charge) with metal ion emissions (colors); reaction rate crucial for proper timing
  • Cooking: Multiple reaction types (Maillard, caramelization, leavening) transform raw ingredients into delicious food
  • All these technologies rely on controlling reaction type, rate, and energy release
  • Understanding chemistry helps us use these technologies more efficiently and sustainably
