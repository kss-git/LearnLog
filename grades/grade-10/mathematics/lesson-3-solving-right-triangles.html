Solving Right Triangles

Grade 10 | Mathematics | Unit 1: Trigonometry

Construction workers need to build a wheelchair ramp that rises 1.5 meters over a horizontal distance of 18 meters. What angle will the ramp make? How long will the ramp be? With trigonometry, you can solve these questions in seconds - and that's exactly what engineers do every day!

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Find missing sides of a right triangle using trig ratios
  • Find missing angles using inverse trig functions
  • Solve complete right triangles given limited information
  • Apply problem-solving strategies to real-world triangle problems

What You'll Learn

Solving a right triangle means finding all unknown sides and angles. You can completely solve any right triangle if you know:

  • One angle (besides the 90° angle) and one side, OR
  • Any two sides

Think of it like detective work: given a few clues, trigonometry helps you discover everything else about the triangle!

Why This Matters

Solving right triangles is essential for countless real-world applications:

  • Construction: Calculating ramp lengths, roof angles, and structural supports
  • Navigation: Determining distances and bearings for ships and aircraft
  • Surveying: Measuring land areas and property boundaries
  • Engineering: Designing bridges, towers, and mechanical systems
  • Video Games: Calculating character movements, camera angles, and projectile paths

Master this skill, and you'll have the mathematical foundation used by architects, engineers, and game developers!

Core Concept: Inverse Trigonometric Functions

Finding Angles from Ratios

We've learned how to find sides using trig ratios. But what if we know the sides and need to find the angle? We use inverse trig functions:

sin⁻¹ (or arcsin): Finds the angle when you know opposite/hypotenuse

cos⁻¹ (or arccos): Finds the angle when you know adjacent/hypotenuse

tan⁻¹ (or arctan): Finds the angle when you know opposite/adjacent

Calculator Note: On calculators, these are often labeled as sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, tan⁻¹ or sometimes arcsin, arccos, arctan. Make sure your calculator is in DEGREE mode, not radians!

The Complete Solving Strategy

Step 1: Draw and label what you know

Step 2: Identify what you need to find

Step 3: Choose the appropriate trig ratio or inverse function

Step 4: Set up the equation and solve

Step 5: Verify your answer makes sense

Example 1: Finding a Missing Angle

Problem: A skateboard ramp has a height of 1.2 meters and a base length of 4 meters. What angle does the ramp make with the ground?

Solution:

  1. Identify known values:
    • Opposite (height) = 1.2 m
    • Adjacent (base) = 4 m
    • Angle θ = ?
  2. Choose the ratio:

    We have opposite and adjacent → Use TAN!

  3. Set up equation:

    tan(θ) = opposite / adjacent

    tan(θ) = 1.2 / 4 = 0.3

  4. Find the angle using inverse tan:

    θ = tan⁻¹(0.3)

    θ ≈ 16.7°

Answer: The ramp angle is approximately 16.7°

Why this works: Since we knew two sides and needed the angle, we used the inverse trig function. This is like "reversing" the normal trig ratio!

Example 2: Solving a Complete Triangle

Problem: A ladder leans against a wall at a 70° angle with the ground. The ladder is 6 meters long. Find the height it reaches on the wall and the distance from the wall base.

Solution:

Given:

  • Angle = 70°
  • Hypotenuse (ladder) = 6 m
  • Want: height (opposite) and base distance (adjacent)

Finding height (opposite):

sin(70°) = opposite / 6

opposite = 6 × sin(70°)

opposite = 6 × 0.940

opposite ≈ 5.64 meters

Finding base distance (adjacent):

cos(70°) = adjacent / 6

adjacent = 6 × cos(70°)

adjacent = 6 × 0.342

adjacent ≈ 2.05 meters

Complete Solution:

  • Height on wall: 5.64 meters
  • Distance from wall: 2.05 meters
  • All angles: 90°, 70°, and 20° (since angles sum to 180°)

Example 3: Using Pythagorean Theorem with Trig

Problem: A right triangle has sides of 8 cm and 15 cm. Find all angles.

Solution:

  1. Find the hypotenuse first:

    Using Pythagorean theorem: a² + b² = c²

    8² + 15² = c²

    64 + 225 = c²

    289 = c²

    c = 17 cm

  2. Find angle θ (opposite 8 cm side):

    sin(θ) = 8 / 17

    θ = sin⁻¹(8/17)

    θ ≈ 28.1°

  3. Find the other angle:

    90° - 28.1° = 61.9°

    (or use cos⁻¹(8/17) to verify)

Complete triangle: sides 8, 15, 17 cm; angles 28.1°, 61.9°, 90°

Quick Check: Test Your Understanding!

Question: A right triangle has an opposite side of 7 cm and an adjacent side of 7 cm. What is the angle θ?

Answer: 45°

Solution:

tan(θ) = opposite / adjacent = 7 / 7 = 1

θ = tan⁻¹(1) = 45°

Special property: When opposite and adjacent are equal, the angle is always 45°! This creates an isosceles right triangle.

Practice Questions

Question 1 (Easy): A triangle has opposite = 3 and hypotenuse = 5. Find the angle using sine.

Answer: θ ≈ 36.9°

Solution:

sin(θ) = 3/5 = 0.6

θ = sin⁻¹(0.6) ≈ 36.9°

Question 2 (Medium): A 10-meter rope is tied from the top of a flagpole to the ground, making a 55° angle with the ground. How tall is the flagpole?

Answer: 8.19 meters

Solution:

Given: hypotenuse = 10 m, angle = 55°

Want: opposite (height)

sin(55°) = height / 10

height = 10 × sin(55°)

height = 10 × 0.819 ≈ 8.19 meters

Question 3 (Medium): A right triangle has sides 5 cm and 12 cm. Find all three angles.

Answer: 90°, 22.6°, 67.4°

Solution:

Step 1: Find hypotenuse: 5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169, so c = 13

Step 2: Find angle: tan(θ) = 5/12, so θ = tan⁻¹(5/12) ≈ 22.6°

Step 3: Other angle = 90° - 22.6° = 67.4°

Question 4 (Hard): An airplane takes off at a 12° angle. After traveling 5 kilometers along its flight path, how high is the plane, and how far has it traveled horizontally?

Answers:

Height: 1.04 km (1,040 meters)

Horizontal distance: 4.89 km

Solution:

Given: hypotenuse = 5 km, angle = 12°

Height = 5 × sin(12°) = 5 × 0.208 ≈ 1.04 km

Horizontal = 5 × cos(12°) = 5 × 0.978 ≈ 4.89 km

Question 5 (Hard): A surveyor needs to measure the width of a river. She measures a 40° angle to a tree on the opposite bank from a point 50 meters downstream from directly across from the tree. How wide is the river?

Answer: 42.0 meters

Solution:

This creates a right triangle where:

- Adjacent (downstream distance) = 50 m

- Angle = 40°

- Opposite (river width) = ?

tan(40°) = opposite / 50

opposite = 50 × tan(40°)

opposite = 50 × 0.839 ≈ 42.0 meters

This is a classic surveying technique called "triangulation"!

Quick Summary

  • Use trig ratios (sin, cos, tan) to find missing sides when you know an angle
  • Use inverse trig functions (sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, tan⁻¹) to find angles when you know sides
  • Make sure your calculator is in DEGREE mode
  • The Pythagorean theorem helps find the third side when you know two sides
  • Angles in a triangle sum to 180°, so if you know one angle, you can find the other
  • Always verify your answer makes sense (angles between 0° and 90°, sides positive)

Real-World Connection: Land Surveying

Before GPS, surveyors used trigonometry to measure land that couldn't be directly measured - like crossing a river, lake, or canyon. This technique is still used today for precise measurements.

The classic method:

  1. Stand at point A and measure the angle to a distant landmark B
  2. Walk a known distance to point C
  3. Measure the angle from C to landmark B
  4. Use trigonometry to calculate the distance to B

This method, called triangulation, was used to:

  • Map entire countries and continents
  • Determine property boundaries
  • Plan roads and railways
  • Design cities and infrastructure

Even today, GPS satellites use triangulation principles - your phone calculates distances to three satellites and uses trigonometry to pinpoint your location!

Challenge Problem

The Mountain Height Problem:

You want to measure the height of a mountain. From your position, you measure the angle of elevation to the peak as 32°. You walk 500 meters directly toward the mountain and measure the angle again - now it's 40°.

Part A: Draw a diagram showing this situation. How many right triangles are involved?

Part B: Let x be the remaining horizontal distance from your second position to the mountain base, and h be the mountain height. Write equations using both angles.

Part C: Solve for the height of the mountain.

Hint for Part B: From the first position, the base distance is (x + 500). From the second position, it's just x.

Both triangles share the same height h!

First triangle: tan(32°) = h / (x + 500)

Second triangle: tan(40°) = h / x

Part A: Two right triangles share the same vertical side (mountain height). One triangle uses the first position (angle 32°), the other uses the second position (angle 40°).

Part B: Setting up equations

From first position: tan(32°) = h / (x + 500)

From second position: tan(40°) = h / x

Part C: Solving

From equation 2: h = x × tan(40°)

Substitute into equation 1:

tan(32°) = [x × tan(40°)] / (x + 500)

0.625 = [x × 0.839] / (x + 500)

0.625(x + 500) = 0.839x

0.625x + 312.5 = 0.839x

312.5 = 0.214x

x ≈ 1,460 meters

Now find h:

h = x × tan(40°)

h = 1,460 × 0.839

h ≈ 1,225 meters

Answer: The mountain is approximately 1,225 meters (1.23 km) tall!

This technique is how surveyors measured mountain heights before modern technology!
