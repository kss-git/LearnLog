Application 1: Architecture and Construction

Architects and engineers use trigonometry constantly to design safe, stable, and beautiful structures.

Roof Design

One of the most common applications is calculating roof dimensions. Roofs need specific angles for:

  • Water drainage: Too flat and water pools; minimum slope varies by climate
  • Snow load: Steeper roofs shed snow better in cold climates
  • Material efficiency: Angles affect how much material you need
  • Aesthetics: Different styles use different angles (modern vs. traditional)

Key formulas:

tan(θ) = height / half-width = h / w

Slope length: s = w / cos(θ) or s = h / sin(θ)

Total roof area = 2 × (s × roof length)

Example 1: Designing a Roof

Problem: An architect designs a house that is 12 meters wide. The roof should have a 35° slope for proper drainage and aesthetics. Calculate:

  • The height of the roof peak above the walls
  • The length of each roof panel
  • If the house is 20 meters long, how much roofing material is needed?

Solution:

Part 1: Height of roof peak

Half-width = 12 / 2 = 6 meters

tan(35°) = height / 6

height = 6 × tan(35°) = 6 × 0.700 ≈ 4.2 meters

Part 2: Length of each roof panel

cos(35°) = 6 / slope

slope = 6 / cos(35°) = 6 / 0.819 ≈ 7.3 meters

Part 3: Total roofing material

Each panel area = 7.3 × 20 = 146 square meters

Two panels = 2 × 146 = 292 square meters

Add 10% for waste: 292 × 1.10 ≈ 321 square meters needed

Complete Solution:

  • Roof peak height: 4.2 meters
  • Each panel length: 7.3 meters
  • Roofing material needed: 321 m²

Application 2: Smartphone Sensors

Your smartphone contains tiny sensors that use trigonometry thousands of times per second to detect orientation, motion, and position.

How Accelerometers Work

An accelerometer measures acceleration forces in three directions (x, y, z). When your phone tilts, gravity creates different forces on each axis:

The Math:

When tilted at angle θ from vertical:

• Perpendicular force = g × sin(θ)

• Parallel force = g × cos(θ)

• Phone calculates: θ = sin⁻¹(perpendicular force / g)

This happens 100-1000 times per second for smooth gaming!

Example 2: Racing Game Steering

Problem: A racing game uses your phone's tilt to steer. The accelerometer detects a sideways force of 4.9 m/s² when gravity is 9.8 m/s². How much is the phone tilted, and how does this translate to steering?

Solution:

Finding the tilt angle:

sin(θ) = sideways force / gravity

sin(θ) = 4.9 / 9.8 = 0.5

θ = sin⁻¹(0.5) = 30°

Game steering interpretation:

Many games use a linear mapping:

  • 0° = straight ahead
  • ±45° = maximum steering
  • 30° tilt = (30/45) × 100% = 67% of maximum steering

Phone tilted 30°, causing 67% steering input

Why this works: The game continuously reads tilt angles using inverse sine and maps them to steering values. This creates the responsive, intuitive controls you experience!

Application 3: Navigation and GPS

GPS (Global Positioning System) uses a constellation of satellites and trigonometry to pinpoint your location within meters.

How GPS Triangulation Works

Your phone measures distances to multiple satellites. Using these distances as "radii" and the satellites' known positions, it uses trigonometry to calculate your exact location.

The Process:

  1. Satellites broadcast their exact position and time signal
  2. Your phone calculates distance to each satellite (time × speed of light)
  3. With 3+ distances, trigonometry solves for your x, y, z coordinates
  4. 4th satellite corrects for clock errors (atomic precision needed!)

Accuracy: Modern GPS: ±5 meters; High-precision: ±1 cm!

Example 3: Navigation Problem

Problem: A ship is sailing and needs to navigate around an island. The captain measures the angle to a lighthouse at 40° from their current heading. After sailing 10 km on the same heading, the angle to the lighthouse is now 75°. How far is the ship from the lighthouse at the second position?

Solution:

Setting up the problem:

Let d = distance from second position to lighthouse

The ship's path, the two lines of sight to the lighthouse, and the perpendicular from the lighthouse to the ship's path form related triangles.

Using the angles:

From second position: We can use the angle difference

The triangle formed has angles: 40°, (75° - 40°) = 35°, and we can find the third: 180° - 40° - 35° = 105°

Applying the Law of Sines:

10 / sin(35°) = d / sin(40°)

d = 10 × sin(40°) / sin(35°)

d = 10 × 0.643 / 0.574

d ≈ 11.2 km

The ship is 11.2 km from the lighthouse

Note: This uses the Law of Sines, an extension of basic trig that works for any triangle, not just right triangles!

Application 4: Video Game Development

Every 3D video game uses trigonometry for graphics rendering, physics, and gameplay mechanics.

Common Uses:

  • Camera angles: Sin and cos rotate the camera around characters
  • Projectile trajectories: Bullets, arrows, and grenades follow parabolic paths calculated with trig
  • Field of view: What you see on screen is determined by viewing angles
  • Lighting: How light reflects off surfaces uses angles and trig ratios
  • Character animation: Joint rotations use sine waves for smooth, natural motion

Example: Shooting Mechanics

When you aim at angle θ with initial velocity v:

• Horizontal velocity = v × cos(θ)

• Vertical velocity = v × sin(θ)

• Range = v² × sin(2θ) / g (maximum at 45°!)

This is why games calculate bullet drop and arrow arc!

Practice Questions

Question 1 (Medium): A wheelchair ramp must rise 0.9 meters with a maximum slope angle of 4.8° (ADA requirement). What is the minimum horizontal length needed?

Answer: 10.7 meters

Solution:

tan(4.8°) = 0.9 / length

length = 0.9 / tan(4.8°)

length = 0.9 / 0.084 ≈ 10.7 meters

This is why ADA-compliant ramps need so much space!

Question 2 (Medium): A drone's camera tilts down at 25° while hovering at 50 meters altitude. How far ahead on the ground is the camera's center point?

Answer: 107.2 meters

Solution:

tan(25°) = 50 / distance

distance = 50 / tan(25°)

distance = 50 / 0.466 ≈ 107.2 meters ahead

Question 3 (Hard): A cell tower is 40 meters tall. For optimal signal, antennas should transmit at angles no greater than 10° below horizontal to reach phones on the ground. What is the maximum horizontal range for this coverage?

Answer: 227 meters radius

Solution:

The 10° angle below horizontal means we use:

tan(10°) = 40 / horizontal distance

horizontal distance = 40 / tan(10°)

horizontal distance = 40 / 0.176 ≈ 227 meters

This is why cities need many cell towers for coverage!

Question 4 (Hard): A video game character jumps at 20 m/s at a 60° angle. How high does the character jump, and how far does it travel horizontally? (Use g = 10 m/s²)

Answers:

Maximum height: 15 meters

Horizontal range: 34.6 meters

Solution:

Vertical velocity = 20 × sin(60°) = 20 × 0.866 = 17.3 m/s

Max height = v² / (2g) = (17.3)² / 20 ≈ 15 meters

Horizontal velocity = 20 × cos(60°) = 20 × 0.5 = 10 m/s

Time in air = 2 × (17.3 / 10) = 3.46 seconds

Range = 10 × 3.46 = 34.6 meters

Question 5 (Very Hard): An architect designs a modern building with a slanted glass facade. The facade is 60 meters tall and leans back at 80° from the ground. To prevent sun glare, they want to add a horizontal overhang at the top that creates a 45° shadow angle at noon. How long should the overhang be?

Answer: 3.15 meters

Solution:

Step 1: Find how far the top of the facade leans out

From vertical: facade angle from vertical = 90° - 80° = 10°

Horizontal offset = 60 × tan(10°) = 60 × 0.176 ≈ 10.6 meters

Step 2: For 45° shadow at ground level

At 45°, the shadow length equals the height: shadow = 60 meters from building base

Step 3: Calculate overhang needed

Total horizontal distance to shadow end = 60 meters

Facade top is already 10.6 meters out

Additional overhang needed = 60 - 60 + tan(10°)×60

Actually, using tan(45°) = 60/x where x is from the facade top point:

Overhang ≈ 3.15 meters

This is a complex real-world problem architects solve regularly!

Real-World Connection: The Burj Khalifa

The world's tallest building (828 meters) is a masterpiece of trigonometric engineering. Every angle was calculated using advanced trigonometry:

  • Wind resistance: The building's Y-shaped floor plan and setbacks use specific angles to reduce wind forces by 25%
  • Structural angles: The core supports are angled precisely to distribute weight evenly
  • Elevator shafts: Slightly angled to account for building sway (up to 1.5 meters at the top!)
  • Foundation depth: Calculated using the building's weight and ground angle of bearing capacity

Without trigonometry:

  • Engineers couldn't calculate safe load-bearing angles
  • The building would collapse under its own weight
  • Wind forces would cause catastrophic failure
  • Foundation design would be impossible

Every modern skyscraper relies on the same trigonometric principles you've learned in this unit!

Challenge Problem

The Solar Panel Optimization Problem:

You're designing a solar panel installation for a house. For maximum efficiency, panels should face the sun at a perpendicular angle. The house is at 40° latitude, and you want optimal angles for both summer and winter.

Given information:

  • Summer: sun reaches 73° above southern horizon at noon
  • Winter: sun reaches 27° above southern horizon at noon
  • Panels are 2 meters × 1 meter each
  • Roof has a south-facing surface at 30° slope

Part A: What angle from horizontal should the panels be mounted for optimal year-round performance? (Hint: average the summer and winter sun angles)

Part B: If panels are mounted at this optimal angle, what's the difference in effective surface area between summer and winter? (Hint: effective area = actual area × cos(angle difference))

Part C: Should the panels be mounted parallel to the roof or at a custom angle? Calculate the efficiency difference.

Hint for Part A: Optimal angle = average of summer and winter sun angles = (73° + 27°) / 2

Hint for Part B: Calculate angle difference between sun and panel for both seasons, then use cos to find effective collection area

Part A: Optimal angle = 50° from horizontal

Average sun angle = (73° + 27°) / 2 = 50°

For perpendicular alignment, panels should match this angle

Part B: Effective area calculation

Panel actual area = 2 × 1 = 2 m²

Summer: angle difference = 73° - 50° = 23°

Effective area = 2 × cos(23°) = 2 × 0.921 = 1.84 m²

Winter: angle difference = 50° - 27° = 23°

Effective area = 2 × cos(23°) = 2 × 0.921 = 1.84 m²

Result: Same effective area both seasons! (This is why we averaged)

Part C: Roof-parallel vs. optimal angle

If parallel to 30° roof:

Summer difference: 73° - 30° = 43°, efficiency = cos(43°) = 0.731 = 73.1%

Winter difference: 30° - 27° = 3°, efficiency = cos(3°) = 0.999 = 99.9%

Average efficiency = (73.1% + 99.9%) / 2 = 86.5%

If at optimal 50° angle:

Both seasons: cos(23°) = 92.1% efficiency

Conclusion: Custom 50° mounting improves efficiency by 5.6% year-round!

This is exactly how solar companies optimize installations!

Real-world impact: For a typical home system (20 panels = 40 m²):

5.6% efficiency gain = ~840 kWh extra per year = $100+ saved annually!
