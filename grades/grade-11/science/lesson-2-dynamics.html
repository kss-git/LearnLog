A 1,500 kg roller coaster car plunges down a 60-meter drop, then whips through a loop-the-loop. How fast is it going at the bottom? Will riders experience weightlessness at the top? These aren't guesses - dynamics gives us exact answers using Newton's laws and force analysis.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Apply Newton's three laws of motion to real-world scenarios
  • Draw and analyze free-body diagrams
  • Calculate net force and predict object acceleration
  • Understand friction, normal force, and tension in systems

What You'll Learn

Dynamics is the study of forces and how they cause motion. While kinematics told us how objects move, dynamics tells us why they move. Every acceleration, every turn, every collision happens because forces are acting.

Master dynamics and you'll understand why seatbelts save lives, how airplanes stay airborne, and why your coffee spills when you brake too hard.

Why This Matters

Dynamics is the foundation of mechanical engineering:

  • Roller Coaster Design: Engineers calculate g-forces to ensure thrilling yet safe rides
  • Car Safety: Crumple zones designed to absorb impact forces during collisions
  • Sports Performance: Optimize force application in sprinting, jumping, throwing
  • Aerospace: Calculate thrust needed for liftoff and orbital maneuvers
  • Structural Engineering: Ensure buildings withstand wind, earthquakes, and loads

Core Concept: Newton's Three Laws

Newton's First Law: Inertia

"An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net force."

Example: When a car suddenly stops, you lurch forward. It's not that a force pushed you - your body wanted to keep moving at the car's original speed (inertia). The seatbelt applies the force to stop you.

Newton's Second Law: F = ma

"The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration."

Formula: ΣF = ma (sum of all forces = mass × acceleration)

Example: A 1,200 kg car accelerates at 3 m/s². The net force is F = (1200)(3) = 3,600 N.

Newton's Third Law: Action-Reaction

"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

Example: When you walk, your foot pushes backward on the ground. The ground pushes forward on your foot with equal force, propelling you forward. These forces are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.

Visual: Free-Body Diagram - Car on a Slope

Key insight: Breaking weight into components lets us analyze motion along the slope. Net force = mg sin θ - F_friction determines if the car accelerates down the slope.

Key Forces in Dynamics

1. Weight (F_g)

The force of gravity pulling an object downward.

Formula: F_g = mg (where g = 9.8 m/s² on Earth)

2. Normal Force (F_N)

The support force perpendicular to a surface. It prevents objects from falling through surfaces.

Note: Normal force equals weight only when on a flat, horizontal surface with no other vertical forces.

3. Friction (F_f)

The force that opposes motion between surfaces in contact.

Formula: F_f = μF_N (where μ is the coefficient of friction)

Static friction: Prevents motion from starting (μ_s)

Kinetic friction: Opposes motion already happening (μ_k)

4. Tension (F_T)

The pulling force transmitted through a rope, cable, or chain.

Note: Tension is the same throughout a massless rope.

5. Applied Force (F_app)

Any external force applied to an object (pushing, pulling, etc.).

Example 1: Accelerating a Car

Problem: A 1,500 kg car accelerates from rest. The engine provides 4,500 N of forward force, while air resistance and rolling friction total 1,200 N backward. What is the car's acceleration?

Solution:

  1. Draw a free-body diagram (mental or on paper):
    • Forward force (engine): 4,500 N →
    • Backward force (resistance): 1,200 N ←
  2. Calculate net force:

    ΣF = 4,500 - 1,200 = 3,300 N forward

  3. Apply Newton's Second Law:

    F = ma

    3,300 = 1,500a

    a = 3,300 / 1,500 = 2.2 m/s²

Answer: 2.2 m/s²

Real-world insight: High-performance cars minimize air resistance (aerodynamic design) and rolling friction (low-resistance tires) to maximize acceleration from engine force.

Example 2: Roller Coaster Loop

Problem: A 600 kg roller coaster car enters a vertical loop (radius = 10 m) at the bottom with a speed of 20 m/s. What is the normal force from the track at the bottom of the loop?

Solution:

  1. Identify forces at the bottom:
    • Weight: F_g = mg = (600)(9.8) = 5,880 N (downward)
    • Normal force: F_N (upward)
    • Net force must point toward center (upward) to maintain circular motion
  2. For circular motion:

    F_net = mv²/r (centripetal force)

    F_net = (600)(20²)/10 = 24,000 N (upward)

  3. Apply Newton's Second Law (vertical direction):

    F_N - F_g = F_net

    F_N - 5,880 = 24,000

    F_N = 29,880 N

Answer: 29,880 N

That's 5× the car's weight! Riders experience about 5g of acceleration.

Real-world insight: Roller coaster designers limit g-forces to 5-6g to ensure safety and rider comfort. Fighter pilots experience similar forces but with special suits to prevent blackouts.

Quick Check: Test Your Understanding

Question: A 50 kg box sits on a horizontal floor. You push it with 200 N of force, but it doesn't move. What is the friction force?

Answer: 200 N (opposite to your push)

Explanation:

Since the box isn't moving, acceleration = 0.

By Newton's Second Law: ΣF = ma = 0

Therefore: F_push - F_friction = 0

F_friction = 200 N (static friction matches your push until you overcome it)

Practice Questions

Question 1 (Easy): If a net force of 20 N acts on a 4 kg object, what is its acceleration?

Answer: 5 m/s²

F = ma → 20 = 4a → a = 5 m/s²

Question 2 (Medium): A 2,000 kg truck decelerates at -3 m/s². What net force is acting on it?

Answer: -6,000 N

F = ma = (2000)(-3) = -6,000 N

Negative indicates the force opposes motion (braking force).

Question 3 (Medium): A 10 kg object hangs from a rope. What is the tension in the rope when the object is at rest?

Answer: 98 N

At rest, tension must equal weight.

T = mg = (10)(9.8) = 98 N

Question 4 (Hard): A 30 kg sled is pulled across snow by a rope at 25° above horizontal with 150 N of force. If friction is 40 N, what is the sled's acceleration?

Answer: 3.2 m/s²

Solution:

Horizontal component of pull: F_x = 150 cos(25°) = 136 N

Net horizontal force: 136 - 40 = 96 N

a = F/m = 96/30 = 3.2 m/s²

Question 5 (Hard): A 1,000 kg car rounds a flat curve (radius = 50 m) at 20 m/s. What friction force is needed to prevent skidding?

Answer: 8,000 N

Solution:

Friction must provide centripetal force.

F_friction = mv²/r = (1000)(20²)/50

F_friction = 400,000/50 = 8,000 N

This is why cars skid on icy curves - ice reduces friction below what's needed.

Quick Summary

  • Dynamics explains how forces cause motion
  • Newton's First Law: Objects resist changes in motion (inertia)
  • Newton's Second Law: F = ma links force, mass, and acceleration
  • Newton's Third Law: Action-reaction pairs are equal and opposite
  • Free-body diagrams help visualize all forces acting on an object
  • Key forces: weight, normal, friction, tension, applied force

Real-World Connection: Car Crash Safety

Modern cars are marvels of force engineering:

  • Crumple Zones: Designed to deform during impact, extending collision time and reducing peak force (F = ma, so larger Δt means smaller F)
  • Airbags: Deploy in 20-30 milliseconds, distributing impact force over a larger area and longer time
  • Seatbelts: Apply restraining force gradually rather than letting you hit the dashboard (sudden, large force)
  • Side-Impact Bars: Reinforce doors to resist lateral collision forces

A 70 kg person in a 50 km/h (14 m/s) crash stopping in 0.1 s experiences: F = ma = m(Δv/Δt) = 70(14/0.1) = 9,800 N. That's 10× their body weight! Crumple zones extend this time to 0.5-1 s, reducing peak forces by 5-10×, which can be the difference between life and death.

Challenge Problem

The Elevator Problem:

You're standing on a bathroom scale (which measures normal force) in an elevator. Your mass is 70 kg.

Part A: What does the scale read when the elevator accelerates upward at 2 m/s²?

Part B: What does it read when accelerating downward at 2 m/s²?

Part C: At what downward acceleration would you feel weightless (scale reads zero)?

Hint: The scale reads the normal force, not your weight.

Free-body diagram: F_N (upward) and F_g (downward)

Net force = ma (positive upward)

F_N - F_g = ma

Part A: Accelerating upward at 2 m/s²

F_N - mg = ma

F_N = m(g + a) = 70(9.8 + 2) = 70(11.8) = 826 N

Scale reads: 826 N (84.3 kg equivalent) - you feel heavier!

Part B: Accelerating downward at 2 m/s²

F_N - mg = m(-a) [negative because downward]

F_N = m(g - a) = 70(9.8 - 2) = 70(7.8) = 546 N

Scale reads: 546 N (55.7 kg equivalent) - you feel lighter!

Part C: Weightless (F_N = 0)

0 - mg = m(-a)

-mg = -ma

a = g = 9.8 m/s² downward

At 9.8 m/s² downward acceleration (free fall), scale reads 0 - you're weightless!

Real-world: This is how NASA creates "zero-gravity" training. Aircraft fly in parabolic arcs with downward acceleration = g, giving astronauts 20-30 seconds of weightlessness.
