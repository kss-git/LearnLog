Graphing Inequalities

Grade 8 | Mathematics | Unit 2: Equations and Inequalities

📊 Imagine you're tracking your gaming rank. You started at rank 50 and gain 3 ranks per win. If you want to reach at least rank 80, how many wins do you need? We can solve this with an inequality, but what if we want to SEE all the possible solutions? That's where graphing comes in - turning math into visual stories!

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Graph inequalities on a number line correctly
  • Use open and closed circles to show included/excluded values
  • Graph compound inequalities (AND/OR situations)
  • Read and interpret inequality graphs
  • Write inequalities from number line graphs

📚 What You'll Learn

Graphing inequalities shows ALL the solutions visually on a number line. Instead of just saying "x > 3", we can show the entire range of valid answers.

Think of it like a thermometer: instead of saying "the temperature is exactly 72°F", we might say "keep the room between 68°F and 75°F". A graph shows that whole acceptable range!

Why graph?

• See the entire solution set at a glance

• Quickly identify if a number is a solution

• Compare multiple inequalities visually

• Communicate ranges more clearly

🌟 Why This Matters

Visual representations of inequalities appear everywhere:

  • Weather Apps: Temperature ranges (55°F - 72°F)
  • Gaming: Level requirements shown as bars or ranges
  • Fitness Trackers: Heart rate zones (cardio: 140-170 bpm)
  • GPS/Maps: Estimated arrival times (12:30 PM - 12:45 PM)
  • Online Shopping: Price range filters ($20 - $50)
  • Health: Normal ranges for blood pressure, BMI, etc.

Data scientists, UX designers, and engineers use graphed inequalities to create intuitive visual displays!

💡 Core Concept: Graphing Rules

Rule #1: Choose the Right Circle

Open Circle (○): Use for < or >

The number is NOT included in the solution

Example: x > 5 (5 itself is not a solution)

Closed/Filled Circle (●): Use for ≤ or ≥

The number IS included in the solution

Example: x ≥ 5 (5 is a solution!)

Rule #2: Shade the Correct Direction

Greater than (> or ≥): Shade to the RIGHT →

All numbers bigger than the boundary

Less than (< or ≤): Shade to the LEFT ←

All numbers smaller than the boundary

Memory Trick:

The arrow points to the solutions! If x > 3, the arrow points right (toward 4, 5, 6...). If x < 3, the arrow points left (toward 2, 1, 0...).

🎨 Visual: The Four Basic Types

✏️ Example 1: Basic Graphing

Problem: Graph the inequality: x ≤ 5

Solution Steps:

  1. Step 1: Draw a number line with 5 marked
  2. Step 2: Use a FILLED circle at 5 (because of ≤)
  3. Step 3: Shade LEFT (because "less than")
  4. Step 4: Add an arrow pointing left

💡 Reading the graph: All numbers to the left of (and including) 5 are solutions: ..., 3, 4, 4.5, 5 all work!

✏️ Example 2: Solving Then Graphing

Problem: Solve and graph: 3x - 4 > 11

Step 1: Solve the inequality

3x - 4 > 11

3x > 15 (add 4 to both sides)

x > 5 (divide by 3)

Step 2: Graph x > 5

  1. Draw number line with 5 marked
  2. Use OPEN circle at 5 (because >)
  3. Shade RIGHT (because "greater than")

💡 Verify: Is x = 6 a solution? 3(6) - 4 = 14, and 14 > 11 ✅ Yes!

Is x = 5 a solution? 3(5) - 4 = 11, and 11 > 11? ❌ No! (That's why we used an open circle)

🔗 Compound Inequalities

Sometimes we need to show a range between two numbers. These are called compound inequalities.

Type 1: AND (Between two values)

Example: -2 < x ≤ 4

This means "x is greater than -2 AND less than or equal to 4"

Solutions: all numbers between -2 and 4 (not including -2, but including 4)

Real-World Example:

"The pool temperature must be between 78°F and 82°F" → 78 ≤ t ≤ 82

🎮 Quick Check: Match the Graph!

Question: Which inequality matches this graph?

A) x > 0
B) x < 0
C) x ≥ 0
D) x ≤ 0

Answer: D) x ≤ 0

Explanation:

• Filled circle at 0 → includes 0 (so ≤ or ≥)

• Arrow points LEFT → less than

• Therefore: x ≤ 0 ✅

📝 Practice Questions

Question 1 (Easy): Write the inequality shown:

Answer: x > 3

Open circle at 3 (not included) and arrow pointing right (greater than)

Question 2 (Medium): Solve and describe how to graph: 2x + 5 ≥ 13

Answer: x ≥ 4

Solution:

2x + 5 ≥ 13

2x ≥ 8

x ≥ 4

How to graph: Filled circle at 4, arrow pointing right

Question 3 (Medium): Graph the compound inequality: -3 ≤ x < 2

Graph description:

• Filled circle at -3 (included)

• Open circle at 2 (not included)

• Thick line connecting them

• No arrows (bounded on both sides)

Solutions: All numbers from -3 to 2, including -3 but not 2

Question 4 (Hard): Solve and graph: -2x + 3 < 11

Answer: x > -4

Solution:

-2x + 3 < 11

-2x < 8 (subtract 3)

x > -4 (divide by -2, FLIP the sign!)

Graph: Open circle at -4, arrow pointing right

Question 5 (Hard): A concert venue holds 500 people. Tickets have been selling at 45 per hour for h hours. At least how many hours until they sell out?

Answer: At least 11.12 hours (so about 12 hours)

Equation: 45h ≥ 500

Solution:

45h ≥ 500

h ≥ 11.11...

Since time must be realistic, at least 12 hours

Graph: Filled circle at 11.11, arrow pointing right

📌 Quick Summary

  • Graphing inequalities shows ALL solutions visually on a number line
  • Open circle (○): Use for < or > (value not included)
  • Filled circle (●): Use for ≤ or ≥ (value included)
  • Arrow direction: Right for >, left for <
  • Compound inequalities: Show ranges between two values
  • Always solve first, then graph the solution

🌍 Real-World Connection: Fitness Heart Rate Zones

Fitness trackers and smartwatches use graphed inequalities to show optimal heart rate zones during exercise!

Example: Cardio Zone for an 18-year-old

Maximum heart rate ≈ 220 - age = 220 - 18 = 202 bpm

Training Zones:

  • Fat Burn: 121 ≤ HR ≤ 141 (60-70% of max)
  • Cardio: 141 < HR ≤ 162 (70-80% of max)
  • Peak: 162 < HR ≤ 182 (80-90% of max)

Your Apple Watch or Fitbit graphs these zones visually! When your heart rate enters a zone, the app highlights it. This helps athletes train effectively by staying in the right intensity range.

Medical devices, climate control systems, and quality control in manufacturing all use graphed inequalities to show acceptable ranges!

🚀 Challenge Problem: The Perfect Temperature

You're designing a smart thermostat for a greenhouse growing tropical plants. The system needs to maintain specific temperature ranges:

  • Daytime: Temperature must be between 75°F and 85°F
  • Nighttime: Temperature must be at least 65°F but no more than 72°F
  • Critical Alert: If temperature drops below 60°F or goes above 90°F

Part A: Write compound inequalities for the daytime and nighttime acceptable ranges.

Part B: Currently, the temperature is 68°F. Is this acceptable during the day? At night?

Part C: The temperature is rising at 2°F per hour. If it's currently 82°F, how many hours until it reaches the critical alert level?

Hint for Part A:

"Between" means greater than one number AND less than another

Use the format: lower ≤ T ≤ upper

Hint for Part C:

Current: 82°F, Rate: +2°F/hour, Critical: 90°F

Set up: 82 + 2h ≥ 90

Part A Answer:

Daytime: 75 ≤ T ≤ 85

Nighttime: 65 ≤ T ≤ 72

Critical zones: T < 60 or T > 90

Part B Answer:

Day: Is 68 between 75 and 85? NO ❌ (too cold!)

Night: Is 68 between 65 and 72? YES ✅ (perfect!)

68°F is acceptable at night but too cold during the day.

Part C Answer: 4 hours

Setup: 82 + 2h ≥ 90

Solution:

82 + 2h ≥ 90

2h ≥ 8 (subtract 82)

h ≥ 4 (divide by 2)

After 4 hours, the temperature will reach 90°F and trigger the critical alert! 🚨

The system should start cooling before then to prevent the plants from overheating.

Bonus visualization: On a number line from 60-95°F:

• Critical zone: 60 to 65 (shaded red, open circles)

• Safe nighttime: 65 to 72 (shaded blue, filled circles)

• Gap: 72 to 75 (unshaded - transition)

• Safe daytime: 75 to 85 (shaded green, filled circles)

• Gap: 85 to 90 (unshaded - transition)

• Critical zone: 90+ (shaded red)
