🎢 You need to be at least 48 inches tall to ride the big roller coaster. Notice it's not exactly 48 inches - it's 48 OR MORE. That's an inequality! Unlike equations where there's one exact answer, inequalities have a whole range of solutions. Let's explore this powerful math tool!

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Understand inequality symbols and what they mean
  • Write inequalities from word problems and real-world situations
  • Solve one-step and two-step inequalities
  • Understand the special rule when multiplying or dividing by negatives
  • Check solutions to verify they work

📚 What You'll Learn

An inequality is like an equation, but instead of saying two things are equal, it shows that one thing is greater than or less than another.

Think of equations as exact measurements (you need exactly 2 cups of flour) and inequalities as ranges (you need at least $20, you must be under 18 years old, etc.)

Equation vs Inequality:

📊 Equation: x = 5 (only ONE solution)

📈 Inequality: x > 5 (INFINITE solutions: 6, 7, 8, 5.1, 100, ...)

🌟 Why This Matters

Inequalities describe most real-world constraints and requirements:

  • Shopping: "I can spend at most $50" (total ≤ 50)
  • Gaming: "Need at least level 10 to enter this dungeon" (level ≥ 10)
  • Health: "Stay under 2000 calories per day" (calories < 2000)
  • Age Restrictions: "Must be 13+ to create an account" (age ≥ 13)
  • Speed Limits: "Don't exceed 65 mph" (speed ≤ 65)
  • Budgets: "Save more than you spend" (savings > spending)

Engineers, economists, and data scientists use inequalities to define constraints, limits, and acceptable ranges!

💡 Core Concept: Inequality Symbols

The Four Main Symbols:

Symbol Meaning Example Read As
< Less than x < 5 "x is less than 5"
> Greater than x > 5 "x is greater than 5"
≤ Less than or equal to x ≤ 5 "x is at most 5"
≥ Greater than or equal to x ≥ 5 "x is at least 5"

Memory Trick: The Alligator Method

Think of the inequality sign as an alligator's mouth - it always opens toward the bigger number!

7 > 3 (the alligator wants to eat the bigger number, 7!)

Solving Inequalities:

Good News: Solving inequalities works almost exactly like solving equations!

ONE BIG RULE: When you multiply or divide both sides by a NEGATIVE number, you must FLIP the inequality sign!

Example of the flip rule:

-2x < 10

Divide both sides by -2: x > -5 (notice < became >!)

✏️ Example 1: Writing Inequalities

Problem: Write an inequality for each situation:

a) You must be at least 16 to get a driver's license.

b) The elevator has a weight limit of 2000 pounds.

c) You need more than 90% to get an A.

Solutions:

  1. a) age ≥ 16

    "At least 16" means 16 or more, so we use ≥

  2. b) weight ≤ 2000

    "Limit of 2000" means 2000 or less, so we use ≤

  3. c) grade > 90

    "More than 90%" means strictly greater, so we use >

    Note: 90% itself is NOT an A, you need more!

💡 Word clues:

  • "At least" → ≥
  • "At most" → ≤
  • "More than" → >
  • "Less than" → <
  • "No more than" → ≤
  • "No less than" → ≥

✏️ Example 2: Solving a Two-Step Inequality

Problem: Solve: 3x + 7 < 22

Solution:

  1. Step 1: Subtract 7 from both sides

    3x + 7 < 22

    3x + 7 - 7 < 22 - 7

    3x < 15

  2. Step 2: Divide both sides by 3

    3x ÷ 3 < 15 ÷ 3

    x < 5

    Note: We divided by a positive number, so the inequality sign stays the same!

  3. Check: Try x = 4 (should work since 4 < 5)

    3(4) + 7 = 12 + 7 = 19, and 19 < 22 ✅

Answer: x < 5

Solutions: 4, 3, 2, 1, 0, -1, -2, ... (any number less than 5!)

✏️ Example 3: The Flip Rule (Negative Division)

Problem: Solve: -4x ≥ 12

Solution:

  1. Divide both sides by -4

    -4x ≥ 12

    -4x ÷ (-4) ≤ 12 ÷ (-4)

    x ≤ -3

    ⚠️ IMPORTANT: The inequality flipped from ≥ to ≤ because we divided by a negative!

  2. Check: Try x = -4 (should work since -4 ≤ -3)

    -4(-4) = 16, and 16 ≥ 12 ✅

Answer: x ≤ -3

Solutions: -3, -4, -5, -6, ... (any number -3 or less!)

💡 Why does it flip? Think about it: if -4x = 12, then x = -3. But since we wanted -4x to be ≥ 12, x needs to be smaller (more negative) than -3, hence x ≤ -3!

🎮 Quick Check: Shopping Budget

Question: You have $60 to spend on games that cost $12 each. You also need to save at least $15 for lunch. How many games can you buy?

Set up: 12g + 15 ≤ 60 (where g = number of games)

Answer: 3 games or fewer

Solution:

12g + 15 ≤ 60

12g ≤ 45 (subtract 15 from both sides)

g ≤ 3.75 (divide by 12)

Since you can't buy 3.75 games, the most you can buy is 3 games! 🎮

Check: 12(3) + 15 = 36 + 15 = $51 ≤ $60 ✅

📝 Practice Questions

Question 1 (Easy): Write an inequality: "You must score at least 70 points to pass the class."

Answer: points ≥ 70

"At least 70" means 70 or more, so we use the ≥ symbol.

Question 2 (Easy): Solve: x + 9 < 15

Answer: x < 6

Solution:

x + 9 < 15

x < 15 - 9 (subtract 9 from both sides)

x < 6

Question 3 (Medium): Solve: 5x - 3 ≥ 17

Answer: x ≥ 4

Solution:

5x - 3 ≥ 17

5x ≥ 20 (add 3 to both sides)

x ≥ 4 (divide by 5)

Question 4 (Hard): Solve: -3x + 5 < 14

Answer: x > -3

Solution:

-3x + 5 < 14

-3x < 9 (subtract 5 from both sides)

x > -3 (divide by -3, flip the sign!)

Check: Try x = 0: -3(0) + 5 = 5, and 5 < 14 ✅

Question 5 (Hard): A cell phone plan costs $40/month plus $0.10 per text. You can spend at most $55 per month. What's the maximum number of texts you can send?

Answer: 150 texts

Equation: 40 + 0.10t ≤ 55

Solution:

40 + 0.10t ≤ 55

0.10t ≤ 15 (subtract 40)

t ≤ 150 (divide by 0.10)

Maximum: 150 texts 📱

📌 Quick Summary

  • Inequalities show relationships where values are not equal
  • Four main symbols: < (less than), > (greater than), ≤ (at most), ≥ (at least)
  • Solve inequalities like equations, using inverse operations
  • FLIP RULE: When multiplying or dividing by a negative, flip the inequality sign
  • Solutions are ranges of values, not just one number
  • Always check your answer with a value from the solution set

To monetize videos on YouTube, creators must meet certain requirements (inequalities!):

  • Subscribers ≥ 1,000
  • Watch hours in past year ≥ 4,000
  • Age ≥ 18 (or have guardian)

850 + 50m ≥ 1,000

50m ≥ 150

m ≥ 3 months

They can apply in at least 3 months! Social media platforms, apps, and games all use inequalities to set requirements and limits.

🚀 Challenge Problem: The Concert Ticket Dilemma

You're organizing a school concert fundraiser. Here's the situation:

  • Venue costs: $800 to rent
  • Ticket price: $15 per person
  • You need to raise AT LEAST $2,000 for the school

Part A: Write an inequality to find how many tickets you need to sell to meet your goal.

Part B: Solve the inequality.

Part C: If the venue holds a maximum of 250 people, can you reach your goal? Explain.

Hint for Part A:

Total raised = (Ticket price × Number of tickets) - Venue cost

This needs to be ≥ $2,000

Part A Answer: 15t - 800 ≥ 2,000

Explanation:

Income from tickets: 15t

Minus venue cost: -800

Must be at least $2,000: ≥ 2,000

Part B Answer: t ≥ 186.67, so need at least 187 tickets

Solution:

15t - 800 ≥ 2,000

15t ≥ 2,800 (add 800 to both sides)

t ≥ 186.67 (divide by 15)

Since you can't sell partial tickets, you need at least 187 tickets!

Part C Answer: YES, you can reach your goal!

Explanation:

• You need to sell at least 187 tickets

• The venue holds 250 people

• Since 187 < 250, you have enough capacity! 🎉

Bonus calculation: If you sell out (250 tickets):

15(250) - 800 = 3,750 - 800 = $2,950 raised!

That's $950 more than your goal! 💰
