🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

📚 What You'll Learn

In real life, objects rarely move in perfect, simple ways. Multiple forces act simultaneously:

  • Friction: Force that opposes motion between surfaces
  • Air resistance (drag): Force that opposes motion through air
  • Normal force: Support force perpendicular to a surface
  • Tension: Force transmitted through ropes, cables, or strings
  • Applied force: Direct push or pull by a person or machine

🌟 Why This Matters

Understanding forces helps engineers and athletes optimize performance:

  • Sports: Shoe design for grip, aerodynamic suits for speed
  • Vehicles: Tire tread patterns, streamlined shapes, braking systems
  • Safety: Airbags, crumple zones, anti-lock brakes
  • Gaming: Realistic physics engines simulate friction and drag
  • Transportation: Trains, planes, cars all optimized for force management

💡 Core Concept: Types of Forces

1. Friction

Friction is the force that opposes motion when two surfaces are in contact.

Key Properties:
  • Direction: Always opposes motion (or attempted motion)
  • Depends on: Surface texture and normal force
  • Types: Static (not moving) and kinetic (moving)
  • Formula: Ffriction = μ × Fnormal (μ = coefficient of friction)

Examples of friction coefficients:

  • Ice on ice: μ ≈ 0.02 (very slippery!)
  • Rubber on dry concrete: μ ≈ 0.7 (good grip)
  • Steel on steel: μ ≈ 0.6
  • Teflon on Teflon: μ ≈ 0.04 (almost frictionless)

2. Air Resistance (Drag)

Air resistance is friction between an object and air molecules.

Key Properties:
  • Increases with speed: Faster movement = more drag
  • Depends on shape: Streamlined shapes have less drag
  • Depends on surface area: Bigger cross-section = more drag
  • Formula (simplified): Fdrag ∝ v² (force proportional to velocity squared)

3. Normal Force

Normal force is the support force perpendicular to a surface.

Key Properties:
  • Direction: Perpendicular (90°) to the surface
  • Magnitude: Equals the force pressing on the surface
  • Example: Book on table - normal force equals book's weight
  • Changes with surface angle (on slopes, normal force decreases)

✏️ Example 1: Calculating Friction

Problem: A 20 kg box sits on a floor. The coefficient of friction between box and floor is 0.4. What force is needed to start the box moving?

Solution:

  1. Find normal force:
    • On a flat surface, normal force = weight
    • Weight = mg = 20 kg × 10 m/s² = 200 N
    • Normal force = 200 N
  2. Calculate maximum static friction:
    • Ffriction = μ × Fnormal
    • Ffriction = 0.4 × 200 N
    • Ffriction = 80 N

Answer: You need more than 80 N to overcome static friction

💡 Key insight: Once the box starts moving, kinetic friction is usually less than static friction, so you need less force to keep it moving than to start it!

✏️ Example 2: Terminal Velocity

Problem: A skydiver jumps from a plane. Initially they accelerate downward at 10 m/s². After falling for a while, they reach terminal velocity (constant speed). Explain what happens using Newton's laws.

Analysis:

Phase 1: Initial Fall

  • Weight (down): Large force from gravity
  • Air resistance (up): Small (moving slowly)
  • Net force: Downward → accelerates down at ~10 m/s²

Phase 2: Speeding Up

  • Weight (down): Same (unchanged)
  • Air resistance (up): INCREASES as speed increases (F ∝ v²)
  • Net force: Still downward but smaller → acceleration decreases

Phase 3: Terminal Velocity

  • Weight (down): Same
  • Air resistance (up): Now EQUALS weight!
  • Net force: ZERO → constant velocity (~120 mph for humans)

At terminal velocity: Weight = Air resistance → Balanced forces → No acceleration (Newton's 1st Law!)

🎮 Quick Check: Test Your Understanding!

Question: You're driving a car at constant speed on a highway. The speedometer shows 100 km/h and doesn't change. What can you conclude about the forces?

Answer: The forces are balanced - engine force equals friction + air resistance.

Explanation:

  • Constant velocity = zero acceleration (Newton's 1st Law)
  • Zero acceleration = zero net force (Newton's 2nd Law: F = ma)
  • Therefore: Engine force forward = Friction + Air resistance backward

This is why you have to keep pressing the gas pedal even at constant speed - you're fighting friction and drag!

📝 Practice Questions

Question 1 (Easy): Why do race cars have spoilers (wings) on the back?

A) To look cool
B) To increase downward force for better traction
C) To reduce air resistance
D) To make the car lighter

Answer: B) To increase downward force for better traction

Spoilers push air upward, and by Newton's 3rd Law, air pushes the car downward. This increases normal force → increases maximum friction → better grip on corners and acceleration!

Question 2 (Medium): A 50 kg sled slides down an icy hill (friction ≈ 0). If the slope angle gives it an acceleration of 5 m/s², what is the net force on the sled?

Answer: 250 N down the slope

Solution: F = ma = 50 kg × 5 m/s² = 250 N

Even though weight = 500 N straight down, only the component along the slope accelerates the sled!

Question 3 (Medium): Explain why runners wear shoes with good tread, using Newton's laws.

Answer: Tread increases friction coefficient between shoe and ground.

Physics explanation:

  • Higher friction → larger force ground can push back on runner (Newton's 3rd Law)
  • Larger backward force on ground → larger forward reaction force
  • More forward force → more acceleration (Newton's 2nd Law: F = ma)

Without friction (like on ice), you can't push backward effectively, so you can't accelerate forward!

Question 4 (Hard): A 1500 kg car traveling at 30 m/s brakes hard. The friction force from brakes is 12,000 N. How far does the car travel before stopping?

Answer: 56.25 meters

Solution:

1. Find acceleration: a = F/m = 12,000 N ÷ 1500 kg = 8 m/s² (deceleration)

2. Use kinematic equation: v² = u² + 2as

0² = 30² + 2(-8)s

0 = 900 - 16s

s = 900/16 = 56.25 m

This is why maintaining safe following distance is critical - stopping takes significant distance!

Question 5 (Hard): A video game character runs at 10 m/s in air, but only 4 m/s underwater. If the character's "engine force" is the same in both, explain the difference using forces.

Answer: Water has much higher drag (resistance) than air.

Physics explanation:

  • At constant (terminal) velocity: Engine force = Resistance force
  • Water is ~800× denser than air → much more resistance
  • Drag increases with velocity squared: Fdrag ∝ v²
  • Same engine force reaches balance with water drag at lower speed

Game physics: Realistic engines simulate this - that's why underwater levels feel "sluggish"!

📌 Quick Summary

  • Friction: Opposes motion between surfaces; F = μN
  • Air resistance: Opposes motion through air; increases with speed²
  • Normal force: Perpendicular to surface; equals weight on flat ground
  • Terminal velocity: When drag equals weight (balanced forces)
  • Force analysis: Identify all forces, find net force, apply F = ma
  • Real applications: Sports equipment, vehicle design, safety systems

🌍 Real-World Connection: Formula 1 Racing

F1 cars are the ultimate example of force engineering:

🏎️ Maximizing Friction (Grip):

  • Wide, soft tires: Increase contact area and friction coefficient
  • Aerodynamic downforce: Wings push car into track (increase normal force → increase friction)
  • At 200 mph: Downforce can be 3× the car's weight!

⚡ Minimizing Air Resistance:

  • Streamlined shape: Smooth airflow reduces drag
  • DRS (Drag Reduction System): Flattens rear wing on straights to reduce drag
  • Every detail matters: Even driver's helmet shape is optimized!

🛑 Braking Forces:

  • Carbon ceramic brakes: Handle extreme temperatures (1000°C+)
  • Deceleration: Can brake at 5-6 g's (5-6 times gravity!)
  • 200 mph → 0 in ~4 seconds: Thanks to massive friction forces

The trade-off: More downforce = better cornering but slower straights (more drag). Engineers constantly balance these forces for each track!

🚀 Challenge Problem

The Bicycle Hill Problem:

You're biking up a hill on a 10° slope. Data:

  • Your mass + bike mass: 80 kg total
  • You pedal with constant force: 300 N
  • Friction + air resistance: 50 N opposing you
  • Gravity component down the slope: ~140 N (≈ 80 kg × 10 m/s² × sin(10°))

Part A: Draw a force diagram showing all forces acting on you and the bike.

Part B: Calculate the net force. Are you accelerating up, down, or moving at constant speed?

Part C: What minimum pedaling force do you need to go up the hill at constant speed?

Part D: If you stop pedaling (force = 0), what is your acceleration down the hill?

Hints:

  • Part B: Net force = Pedaling force - (friction + air + gravity component)
  • Part C: For constant speed, net force must be zero
  • Part D: Use F = ma with the net force when pedaling = 0

Part A: Force Diagram

Forces parallel to slope:

  • Pedaling: 300 N (up slope) ↗
  • Gravity component: 140 N (down slope) ↙
  • Friction + air: 50 N (down slope) ↙

Forces perpendicular to slope: Normal force and weight component (balanced)

Part B: Net Force = +110 N up the slope

Calculation:

Net force = 300 N - 140 N - 50 N = 110 N (up slope)

Since net force is positive (up hill), you're accelerating upward!

Acceleration: a = F/m = 110 N ÷ 80 kg = 1.375 m/s²

Part C: Minimum force = 190 N

For constant speed: Net force = 0

Pedaling force = Gravity component + Friction

= 140 N + 50 N = 190 N

Part D: Acceleration = 2.375 m/s² down the hill

Calculation:

Net force (no pedaling) = 0 - 140 N - 50 N = -190 N (down slope)

a = F/m = -190 N ÷ 80 kg = -2.375 m/s² (negative = down slope)

You'd roll backward, accelerating downhill! This is why it's hard to stop on a steep hill without brakes! 🚴
