💡 Core Concept 1: Point of View (POV)

First Person POV (I, me, my)

What it is: The narrator is a character in the story, speaking directly to you.

Example: "I crept down the dark hallway, my heart pounding. I could hear footsteps behind me."

Best for: Personal connection, emotional depth, unreliable narrators

Used in: The Hunger Games, Percy Jackson, Minecraft: Story Mode

Second Person POV (You, your)

What it is: The reader IS the character - uncommon but powerful.

Example: "You open the chest. Inside, you find three items. You must choose one."

Best for: Interactive fiction, choose-your-own-adventure, game tutorials

Used in: Text adventures, choice-based games, some experimental fiction

Third Person Limited (He, she, they - one character's thoughts)

What it is: An outside narrator tells the story but focuses on one character's perspective.

Example: "Sarah heard the door creak. Her pulse quickened. Someone was in the house."

Best for: Balance between intimacy and flexibility

Used in: Harry Potter, most modern YA novels, The Last of Us

Third Person Omniscient (All-knowing narrator)

What it is: The narrator knows everything about all characters and events.

Example: "While Sarah searched the downstairs, the intruder hid upstairs, equally terrified. Neither knew they were just two scared neighbors."

Best for: Complex plots with multiple storylines, epic fantasy

Used in: Lord of the Rings, Game of Thrones, classic literature

💡 Core Concept 2: Pacing

Pacing controls the speed of your story. Think of it like music - you need fast parts, slow parts, and variation to keep things interesting.

Fast Pacing

Techniques:

  • Short sentences and paragraphs
  • Action verbs and minimal description
  • Quick dialogue exchanges
  • Time skips between scenes

Example: "Run! I sprinted. Jumped the fence. Didn't look back. The sound grew closer."

Best for: Action scenes, chase sequences, climactic moments

Slow Pacing

Techniques:

  • Longer, flowing sentences
  • Detailed descriptions
  • Internal thoughts and reflection
  • Exposition and world-building

Example: "The garden stretched before her, peaceful in the afternoon sun. Roses bloomed in carefully tended rows, their petals soft as silk, their fragrance sweet and heavy in the warm air."

Best for: Character development, emotional moments, setting scenes

💡 Core Concept 3: Foreshadowing & Flashbacks

Foreshadowing

What it is: Hints about what's coming later in the story.

Subtle Example: "She pocketed the key, not knowing it would save her life."

Obvious Example: "If only I'd known what tomorrow would bring, I never would have left the house."

Pro Tip: Best foreshadowing is noticed on a re-read, not the first time!

Flashbacks

What it is: Jumping back to show past events.

Signaling Example: "The smell of cinnamon took me back to that morning, three years ago..."

Warning: Don't overuse! Flashbacks can slow pacing.

Best Practice: Use flashbacks to reveal crucial information the reader needs NOW.

✏️ Example: Applying Narrative Techniques

Scenario: Writing an Among Us fanfic where the imposter is revealed.

Setup (Slow Pacing + Foreshadowing):

"Red had been quiet all game. Too quiet. I watched them move through electrical, their movements smooth and confident. Something about the way they paused at the door made my stomach twist. I'd seen that same pause right before Blue disappeared."

Techniques used:

  • Third person limited (narrator's thoughts only)
  • Slow pacing with detailed observation
  • Foreshadowing (the pause detail)

Climax (Fast Pacing + Tension):

"The lights cut out. Darkness. A scream. I ran. Emergency button. Slammed it. Lights blazed. Red stood over Green's body. Our eyes met. Everyone knew."

Techniques used:

  • Fast pacing with short sentences
  • Sentence fragments for urgency
  • Action verbs (cut, slammed, blazed)

🎯 Practice Exercises

Exercise 1: POV Transformation

Take this sentence and rewrite it in all four POVs:

"The player discovered a secret room filled with treasure."

Try: First person, second person, third limited, third omniscient

Exercise 2: Pacing Practice

Write two versions of this scene:

A character enters a spooky abandoned house.

Version A: Slow pacing (build atmosphere)

Version B: Fast pacing (immediate danger)

Exercise 3: Foreshadowing

Write the opening paragraph of a story where you subtly foreshadow that:

The main character's best friend is actually a time traveler.

Challenge: Make it subtle enough that readers won't catch it immediately!

🚀 Pro Tips

  • Mix it up: Don't use the same pacing throughout - vary between fast and slow
  • POV consistency: Once you pick a POV, stick with it (switching confuses readers)
  • Foreshadowing subtlety: Plant 3-4 hints, but make only 1-2 obvious
  • Flashback rule: Only flashback if the past event is MORE interesting than the present
  • Study your favorites: Read your favorite fanfics or books and identify their techniques
  • Game writing: Visual novels and RPGs are great examples of POV and pacing
